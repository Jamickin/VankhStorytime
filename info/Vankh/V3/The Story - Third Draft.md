# **Chapter 1: The Principle of Mentalism** - "The All is Mind; The Universe is Mental"

*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

Pro synchronized his breathing pattern with the deep resonant VVVVVANKH that pulsed through the shuttle's cabin. Around him, the few other passengers performed the same respiratory ritual, their enlarged bi-pupiled eyes half-closed in concentration. The ambient lighting dimmed to a softer hue, designed to ease the transition into FTL travel.

"Approaching FTL initiation," announced the automated system. "Pulse synchronization recommended. Initiation commences on the third Vankh."

Pro's specialized tool bag rested between his feet, the coupling diagnostic equipment inside worth more than what most technicians earned in a full Vankh-Sett. The corporate logo on his shoulder patch—a stylized "C" with three radiating lines representing Cicillian Industries—seemed oddly out of place on this unmarked independent vessel. These ships had been multiplying throughout the sector, operating in the margins of corporate oversight where desperate people paid premium rates for untraceable transport.

The credits from this coupling repair would buy medicine for his parents. Their latest message had been increasingly desperate as the Church of Purity tightened its grip on the Helix System, requiring "spiritual certification" before dispensing even basic medical supplies. His mother's condition was deteriorating, though she insisted it was just implant rejection—nothing the temporal stabilizers couldn't handle.

*Paranoid delusions,* Pro reminded himself, pushing away the memory of her frantic warnings during their last conversation. *The implant rejection is affecting her cognitive functions.* 

The crystal she'd given him sat in his tool bag's secure compartment, unexamined. She'd pressed it into his hands with wild eyes, speaking of "suppressed histories" and "cosmic experiments," insisting he needed to understand what was really happening with the Vankh network. Classic symptoms of neural desynchronization—the past and present blurring together, creating elaborate conspiracy theories from fractured memories.

He'd taken the crystal to humor her, but examining it would only feed her delusions. What she needed was proper medical care, not validation of her paranoid fantasies about corporate cover-ups and reality manipulation.

*Right lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

He felt the familiar tension in his strengthened tendons as his body prepared for acceleration. This would be his fourth transit this Vankh-Sett—pushing the recommended safety limit. The subtle vibration from the FTL core beneath the passenger cabin resonated at a frequency slightly out of sync with the true Vankhs. After years working with drive cores, Pro could feel the dissonance in his chest cavity—a professional hazard that sometimes left him with temporal migraines after long shifts.

"First time on an indie transport?" 

Pro looked across the narrow aisle to see a corporate manager with standard neural enhancement ports gleaming at her temples. Her suit marked her as mid-level administration, probably thinking this side trip made her adventurous.

"Ship's running hot," Pro answered, keeping his voice low while tapping his technician's badge. "Coupling needs adjustment. Nothing dangerous, but they're pushing for speed."

She nodded nervously, her enhancement ports flickering as they processed his credentials. "Is that what you're here for? Repairs?"

"Unauthorized consultation," Pro confirmed. "Independent operators always try to squeeze extra performance from outdated equipment. Never understanding how the harmonics affect long-term integrity."

What he didn't mention was how his diagnostic readings had been showing increasingly bizarre patterns lately. Mathematical relationships so precise they seemed designed rather than naturally occurring. Harmonic frequencies that matched no known physical constants yet somehow maintained perfect stability. The kind of readings that suggested conscious control rather than chaotic physics.

*Professional paranoia,* he told himself. *Too much time listening to mother's theories.*

VVVVVANKH!

The second pulse triggered the final breathing sequence. Pro thought of his parents, trapped in what was once the prestigious Helix Habitat, now proclaimed the divine birthplace of the Prophet. The Church of Purity had transformed his childhood home into a fortress against "technological corruption," their zealots patrolling the curved streets where he once played.

The last time he'd visited, his father's face had aged decades in just a few Vankh-Setts, worn down by the daily "purification rituals" now required of all habitat residents. His mother's eyes had lost their spark, the cortical enhancement scars on her temples now disguised under mandatory head coverings.

"We're managing," his father had whispered, glancing nervously at the door. "Your mother... the condition is progressing. She has theories about the corporate databases. Thinks she's found something important."

"Implant rejection affects cognitive function," Pro had replied, the standard medical explanation. "Creates false pattern recognition, conspiracy ideation. The neural pathways can't distinguish between correlation and causation."

But his father's expression had suggested something more than medical concern. Fear, maybe. Or recognition.

VVVVVANKH!

The third pulse arrived. Pro completed his breathing cycle as the FTL drive fully engaged. The familiar sensation of reality stretching washed over him—the beginnings of Dilation Hysteria held carefully at bay by his disciplined respiratory pattern. His enhanced eyes adjusted to the temporal distortion, the specialized pupil structure filtering the visual anomalies that accompanied FTL travel.

Across the aisle, the corporate manager faltered in her breathing sequence, her enhancement ports sparking as they struggled to compensate. Pro reached over instinctively, tapping her wrist in the rhythm she needed to follow. Sweat beaded across her forehead as she fought to synchronize, the early symptoms of Dilation Hysteria flickering across her features.

"Thank you," she whispered once she'd found the pattern. "I don't usually travel without stabilization fields."

"Corporate shuttles spoil you," Pro replied. "Indies run raw."

As the shuttle slipped into the deeper phase of faster-than-light travel, Pro activated his portable diagnostic unit, ostensibly checking his equipment but actually running passive scans on the ship's coupling system. The readings populated his display in neat columns of data—temperature variations, harmonic frequencies, resonance patterns.

The patterns were wrong.

Not dangerous wrong, but mathematically impossible wrong. The harmonic resonance showed perfect golden ratio relationships across seventeen different frequency bands. The thermal distribution followed Fibonacci sequences that should never occur in chaotic mechanical systems. The temporal displacement readings created geometric progressions that demanded conscious design.

Pro stared at the data, his enhanced neural pathways processing the implications faster than his conscious mind wanted to accept them. This wasn't random physics creating pretty patterns. This was engineered precision disguised as natural phenomena.

*Mother's paranoia is contagious,* he told himself, but the readings didn't lie. Whatever was happening with the Vankh network, the mathematical relationships were too perfect for coincidence. Too precise for nature. Too controlled for chaos.

He closed the diagnostic display before his training could force him to investigate further. Some questions were better left unasked, especially when the answers might prove his supposedly delusional mother had been right all along.

The shuttle continued its journey through twisted space-time, its course plotted by navigation systems that corrected for stellar drift patterns that—according to the readings Pro had just seen—didn't actually exist. The FTL drive hummed with harmonics that matched theoretical perfection rather than mechanical imprecision. The Vankh pulse that enabled their impossible journey beat with rhythmic precision that suggested purpose rather than phenomenon.

*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

Pro maintained his breathing pattern and tried not to think about crystal data crystals, impossible mathematics, or the growing certainty that his mother's "delusions" might be the most accurate perception of reality anyone had achieved in generations.

The universe hummed around him with perfect, terrible precision, and for the first time in his career, Pro wondered if the technology he serviced was serving something else entirely.

VVVVVANKH!

The pulse resonated through his consciousness, no longer just a navigation beacon but a question he wasn't ready to answer: *In a universe where mind created matter, whose mind was doing the creating?*

*The shuttle flew on through the infinite dark, carrying its passengers toward destinations they thought they had chosen, following routes that had been planned before they were born, synchronized to a rhythm that counted down to something none of them understood.*
