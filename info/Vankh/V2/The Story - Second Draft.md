# Chapter 1: Rhythm

Pro's elongated fingers drummed against his thigh, counting the seconds until departure. The acrid smell of recycled air, tinged with the metallic tang of drive lubricant, filled his nostrils. Around him, passengers shifted nervously as the overhead lights dimmed from harsh fluorescence to a softer blue glow.

"Commencing FTL initiation," announced the automated system, its synthetic voice oddly melodic against the background hum of the engines. "Pulse synchronization recommended."

*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse*

He recited the technique in preparation for the Vankh Pulse, the familiar mantra settling into his muscles like an old friend. Around him, other passengers performed the same respiratory ritual, their bi-pupiled eyes half-closed in concentration. Some whispered the pattern aloud, their voices creating a quiet chorus of breath work.![[Initial Render Shuttle.png]]

Pro scanned the passenger cabin with practiced efficiency. Three rows ahead, a cluster of Purist adherents huddled together, their faces tense with the effort of following the breathing pattern. Their appearance marked their philosophical stance—elongated fingers and bi-pupiled eyes like everyone else, but deliberately stripped of all technological enhancements. Several bore the telltale scarring where implants had been surgically removed, leaving distinctive pockmarked patterns on temples and wrists. A Cicillian corporate policy officer sat nearby, his silver-threaded uniform marking him as regulatory compliance, cybernetic enhancements visibly integrated along his jawline and wrists. He watched the Purists with thinly veiled disdain, the kind reserved for those who chose philosophical objection over practical advancement.

*Right lung. Pause. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Exhale. Reverse*

The specialized tool bag beside him clinked softly as the shuttle vibrated, its coupling diagnostic equipment worth more than what most technicians earned in a full Vankh-Sett. Pro ran a protective hand over its worn surface, feeling each scratch and dent like a memory.

"Damn corporation," he muttered, exhaling slowly. The stylized "C" with three radiating lines on his shoulder patch—Cicillian Industries' ubiquitous logo—seemed to mock him with its pristine stitching against the unmarked shuttle he'd boarded. These independent vessels had been multiplying throughout the sector recently, their hulls bearing the scars of hasty repairs and unofficial modifications as they operated beyond Cicillian's watchful gaze. His official assignment had ended two stops before, but instead of returning to the corporate hub on Nexus Station, he'd quietly accepted three more off-the-books coupling inspection jobs.

He needed those credits. His parents' last message still echoed in his mind—their voices tight with fear as they described how the Church of Purity had tightened its grip on medical supplies in the Helix Habitat. "Spiritual certification" they called it, a bureaucratic barrier between the sick and their treatment. The Church had banned everything from crude Motion Enhancers to complex Sensory Augmentations, declaring all technological assistance a "tainting of the flesh." The same fanatics who had once praised such advancements now labeled them heretical.

Pro's mind drifted to three Vankh-Setts ago, when he'd received the message that changed everything. He'd been in the midst of a routine coupling inspection on a Cicillian heavy transport when his personal communication device had chimed with an urgent family priority tag. The holographic projection of his father's face had appeared, lined with new worry creases that hadn't been there during Pro's last planetside visit.

"The Prophet's Voice has announced emergency measures," his father had said, voice lowered though they were on a secure channel. "They're calling it 'spiritual reclamation'—but it's a purge, son. They're systematically removing all corporate influence from the habitat. Your mother..." His father's voice had cracked then. "They've closed the archives. Anyone with cortical implants is being required to register for 'spiritual rehabilitation.'"

Pro had known what that meant. His mother, with her archivist's enhancements—memory processors, data sifters integrated directly into her neural pathways during her forty years of service to the Helix Historical Repository—would be among the first targeted.

*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung...*

The familiar tension spread through his strengthened tendons as his body prepared for acceleration. This would be his fourth transit this Vankh-Sett—pushing well beyond the recommended safety limit. "Those who Travel, Earn. Those who Earn, Survive," went the corporate creed, a hollow slogan repeated by managers who rarely left planetside comforts.

He remembered how proud he'd been when first accepted to the Cicillian Academy of Engineering. In those days—before the Church of Purity had gained political power—an appointment to the Academy had been the pinnacle of achievement for a habitat-born child. His parents had celebrated for days, his father especially beaming with pride. After all, it had been his father who had inspired Pro's fascination with drive technology, bringing home technical manuals from his engineering shifts at the Cicillian maintenance facility in the outer habitat ring.

"You'll have more opportunities than I did," his father had said during Pro's departure celebration. "Full engineering clearance, not just maintenance routines." His father had tapped his own damaged leg, the faulty Motion Enhancer visible beneath his uniform trousers. It had been that defective enhancement—an early model prone to neural rejection—that had ended his father's career. Cicillian had "retired" him five Vankh-Setts before standard age, deeming him "efficiency-compromised" despite his superior technical knowledge.

The academy years had been filled with rigorous indoctrination—not just technical training, but inescapable corporate philosophy embedded into everything from meal supplements to sleep-learning modules. Pro remembered those late-night sessions, the educational feeds bypassing conscious thought to implant "historical context" directly into the students' memory centres.

"The Cicillian Corporation's founding mandate of 2178 established humanity's covenant with the Vankh network," the modules had repeated night after night. "Through dedicated sacrifice and innovation, our forebears harnessed the pulse that now allows civilisation to span the stars."

It was only years later that Pro had discovered the inconsistencies in these "histories"—the suspiciously convenient narratives that justified Cicillian's monopoly over drive technology, the careful omission of competing theories about the Vankh network's origins. 

"What a joke," Pro thought, as a subtle vibration from the FTL core beneath the passenger cabin resonated through the deck plates, its frequency trademarked as out of sync with the true Vankhs. After years working with drive cores, he could feel the dissonance in his chest cavity—a professional hazard that sometimes left him with temporal migraines that lasted for days. He felt the warning signs already building behind his specialized eyes, a pressure like distant thunder.

The specialization ceremony remained vivid in his memory. Final-year Academy students gathered in the grand auditorium, the air thick with anticipation as corporation assessors announced assignments that would define their entire careers. Pro had known his aptitude scores placed him at the top percentile for full drive engineering—the elite track that would grant access to the most closely guarded secrets of Vankh technology.

"Candidate P-R-0-4-7-9," the assessor had called, using his full designation rather than the abbreviated name he preferred. "Drive Coupling Specialist."

The words had fallen like blows. Not full engineering access. Not theoretical design work. Coupling specialist—a crucial role, certainly, but limited to a single component system. His protests had been met with corporate platitudes about "optimal resource allocation" and "specialized excellence."

Later, he'd discovered the truth from an instructor who'd taken pity on him: his father's "retirement" had flagged Pro's genetic profile. Corporate risk assessment algorithms had deemed him potentially "unstable for sensitive information access" based on nothing more than hereditary association.

*...Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse*

"Pardon me." The voice broke his concentration. A slender woman with chromed eye implants leaned across the narrow aisle, her fingers gripping the armrest so tightly that her knuckles had gone pale. The implants caught the blue light, reflecting it in prismatic patterns across her face. "This is my first time traveling independent. Is that... normal?" She gestured toward the rattling seats and buckles, wincing as a particularly strong vibration shuddered through the cabin.

Pro glanced at her corporate attire—too pristine, too new. The faint scent of expensive synthetic fabric confirmed his suspicion: executive class, unused to anything less than luxury transport.

"Ship's running hot," he answered, keeping his voice low enough that it wouldn't carry beyond their conversation. "Coupling needs adjustment. Nothing dangerous, but they're pushing for speed." He tapped his corporate badge with a conspiratorial tap. "Don't mention I said anything."

The woman's chromed eyes widened, iris-enhancements contracting nervously. She nodded, reaching into her coat pocket to produce a small lanyard badge. "Stationary Specialist GRESS-358," she introduced herself, her diction precise and clipped in the way of those who rarely ventured beyond corporate enclaves.

Pro studied the standard Cicillian Industries tag as she held it out. Upper management slumming it on an independent vessel? Unusual. Perhaps she thought it adventurous, or more likely, she'd discovered the significant price difference between corporate and independent transport. He made a mental note of this curious encounter.

"Drive Coupling Specialist PRO-112," he replied, handing back her credentials. His own voice carried the slight roughness common among technicians who spent too much time inhaling drive coolant. "May the Pulse guide you."

"May the Pulse guide you," she echoed, settling back into her seat with visible relief, as though his technical certification alone might protect her from the ship's questionable maintenance.

An older man in the row opposite—clearly a Purist adherent by his simple clothing and the religious medallion visible at his collar—scoffed audibly at their exchange. "Corporate blessings," he muttered just loudly enough to be heard, "as if the Pulse belongs to Cicillian."

Pro had encountered this attitude often enough to recognize a Purist sympathizer, though not a full convert—those wouldn't dare travel on FTL vessels at all, considering it blasphemous interference with the divine rhythm. This man represented the dangerous middle ground—resentful of corporate control but not yet radicalized to violent rejection of technology.

"The Pulse guides all who respect its rhythm," Pro replied neutrally, the corporate-approved response to religious provocations. The man turned away with a dismissive grunt.

Gress leaned closer, her voice barely audible. "Are there many..." she hesitated, searching for a diplomatic term, "...adherents traveling independent these days?"

Pro nodded slightly. "Corporate rates have tripled, as you know, since the Helix situation. Many can't afford the prerequisites for standard transport." He didn't add that these Purist sympathizers often served as information couriers between growing cells throughout corporate space, their anti-scanning garments making them immune to the standard security checkpoints.

Pro's eyes drifted to his own hands, the subtle technological enhancements visible to those who knew what to look for. Beyond his naturally elongated fingers—evolved for dexterity through generations of space travel—were the artificial additions: the barely perceptible dermal reinforcement grafted into his skin that protected against drive radiation, and the neural interfaces embedded at his wrist joints. Not choices he had made, but prerequisites for his specialty, mandated and implemented during his first year at the Academy.

The shuttle's vibrations intensified, and Pro's trained ear detected the harmonic undertones in the drive's pulse. The coupling would show accelerated corrosion when he inspected it later—these independent operators always pushed their equipment too hard, squeezing extra speed from outdated technology. They never understood how subtle misalignments affected drive integrity, how each forced acceleration shortened the component's lifespan exponentially.

Beyond the viewport, space itself began to distort as the FTL drive powered up to full capacity. Stars stretched into lines of light, their colors shifting into ranges beyond normal perception. Only Pro's specialized training allowed him to track these changes without the protective filters that corporate vessels automatically deployed. The independent shuttle offered no such luxuries—passengers either possessed appropriate training or suffered the psychedelic effects of unfiltered FTL distortion.

When not fulfilling his official corporate duties, Pro had built a quiet reputation among independent pilots who either couldn't afford the standard levy for a corporate specialist or couldn't risk the legal complications of having an official inspection. This particular shuttle was a fascinating hybrid—Cicillian hull married to a third-party drive system, a technical heresy that corporate inspectors would condemn immediately.

His last illicit inspection job had nearly ended in disaster when a corporate security patrol had conducted a surprise dock check. Pro had barely avoided detection by hiding in a maintenance shaft for six hours, breathing through emergency filters while the officers meticulously cataloged every component on the independent vessel. The captain had later revealed that corporate sabotage of independent vessels had become increasingly common—a pattern that coincided with the Church of Purity's growing influence.

*VVVVVANKH!*

The Vankh Pulse thundered through the cabin, the sound more felt than heard—a vibration that seemed to resonate in the marrow of his bones. The pulse triggered the final breathing sequence, and Pro's thoughts turned involuntarily to his parents.

They were still living in what had once been the prestigious Helix Habitat, now proclaimed the divine birthplace of the Prophet. The Church of Purity had transformed his childhood home into a fortress against what they called "technological corruption." Zealots patrolled the curved streets where he had once played, their robes swishing against the polished floors as they searched for forbidden devices. Priests conducted daily ceremonies in the Lux-Zones he'd once frequented with his mother, the spaces now heavy with incense meant to "purify" the air of technological taint.

His mother had once overseen the habitat's historical archives, her cortical enhancements allowing her to process and catalog thousands of documents daily. She had been renowned for her encyclopedic knowledge of pre-Vankh exploration records—a subject that had mysteriously become restricted shortly before the Church gained power. Now she was forced to hide her enhancements beneath ceremonial head coverings, the implants slowly failing without proper maintenance. These weren't simple augmentations but complex neural interfaces that had integrated with her natural brain tissue over decades of use.

During Pro's last planetside visit, she had shown signs of neural degradation—moments of confusion, temporal displacement as her aging enhancements conflicted with her natural cognitive processes. The medications she needed were classified as "spiritually corrupting" by the new authorities, available only through increasingly dangerous black market channels.

The last time he'd smuggled himself planetside, the anti-aircraft batteries had nearly shot his shuttle down. He could still taste the acrid burn of emergency coolant that had flooded the cabin during his hasty escape.

His father's face seemed to age Vankh-Setts every time he visited, worn down by the daily "purification rituals" now required of all habitat residents and the stress of managing the underground rebellion. The Cicillian corporation had abandoned him long before the Purists took power, cutting off his enhancement maintenance when he was deemed no longer cost-effective. Now his movement enhancer caused constant pain as it degraded, fused to his bone structure through osseointegration—impossible to remove without corporate medical facilities that the Purists had dismantled. The jagged line where metal met bone was visibly inflamed, the skin mottled purple and red from the chronic rejection his immune system mounted against the neglected technology.

"We're managing," his father had whispered during their last meeting, glancing nervously at the door as voices passed in the corridor outside. "The Prophet's Voice announced another cleansing ceremony next pulse. They're searching for hidden tech." His father had passed Pro a small data crystal, its surface unmarked. "Your mother wanted you to have this—archive records she managed to save before they locked everything down. Said it's something about the early Vankh discoveries that doesn't match corporate history."

Pro hadn't had a chance to examine the crystal yet. It remained hidden in a secret compartment of his tool bag, a potentially treasonous possession that could end his career—or worse—if discovered during a corporate security scan.

Outside the viewport, reality itself began to fragment as the FTL transition intensified. The black void between stars took on texture—rippling patterns that most were trained to ignore but that Pro had always found fascinating. These distortion fields resembled the Vankh spheres themselves—perfect black orbs that dotted settled space, emitting the pulses that made faster-than-light travel possible. No one understood exactly what the spheres were or why they generated the pulse, only that civilization had learned to harness their rhythm for interstellar travel.

VVVVVANKH!

The second pulse hit harder than the first, and Pro sank deeper into his seat. The familiar sensation of reality stretching washed over him—a feeling like being pulled in all directions simultaneously while remaining perfectly still. The beginnings of Dilation Hysteria pressed at the edges of his consciousness, held carefully at bay by his disciplined respiratory pattern. His specialized training combined with the Cicillian neural calibrators at his temples allowed him to adjust to the temporal distortion, filtering the visual anomalies that poured through the viewports—streaking stars and fluctuating color spectrums that could drive the unprepared mad. The natural sensitivity of his bi-pupiled eyes—evolved over generations for detecting subtle light variations in space—worked in perfect synchronization with the technological enhancements to process what would otherwise be overwhelming sensory input.

Beyond the standard visual distortions of FTL travel, Pro noticed something unusual in the void—a secondary ripple pattern that seemed to echo the primary pulse. In his eight Vankh-Setts of transit work, he'd never observed this phenomenon. It resembled the harmonic undertones he sometimes detected in poorly calibrated couplings, but occurring in space itself rather than mechanical systems.

"I hope he knows what he's doing..." Pro muttered, closing his eyes and synchronizing his breathing more deliberately.

*Right lung. Pause. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Exhale. Reverse*
*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse*

A strangled gasp from across the aisle pulled his attention back to the present. The corporate woman—Gress—was struggling with the reality shift, her breathing pattern faltering as panic overtook her training. A sheen of sweat glistened on her forehead, and her chromed eyes had dilated fully, reflecting the chaotic light patterns in terrifying amplification. The early symptoms of Dilation Hysteria flickered across her features as her perception destabilized.

Around them, the Purist passengers were faring even worse—the man with the religious medallion had gone completely rigid, his eyes rolled back showing only whites. The crude scar tissue where he had removed his neural stabilizers left him particularly vulnerable to transit effects. A transit attendant moved efficiently through the cabin, administering emergency stabilizers to those showing the most severe symptoms. One of the corporate compliance officers was documenting each case on a wrist-mounted scanner, the device integrated directly into his forearm with no visible seam between flesh and technology—a stark contrast to the Purists with their deliberate rejections and removed enhancements.

Her vision must be swimming, Pro realized, watching the telltale signs in Gress. Cascades of shapes flowing across her eyes, her prior conversation with him replaying as both déjà vu and memory simultaneously, clouding her mind with temporal confusion. The worst would come next—vertigo, followed by the violent Snap-Back that could leave permanent neural damage.

"Amateur," Pro whispered, keeping his eyes fixed on her distress. He began tapping his foot against the deck plates, creating a rhythm she could follow. The metallic click-click-click cut through the disorienting hum of the drives, offering a lifeline of pattern.

Just before the Snap-Back could take hold, her breathing steadied, following his lead. Her vision cleared gradually, the panic receding from her features. After a tense moment that seemed to stretch eternally, the shuttle shuddered one final time as reality fully transitioned to FTL state.

The view outside the viewport had transformed completely. No longer the star-filled blackness of normal space, FTL transit created its own unique visual environment—a swirling tunnel of blue-shifted light at the periphery surrounding an absolute void at the center. Corporate scientists called it the "transit aperture," but technicians like Pro knew it by a different name: the Vankh's Eye. Legend among drive workers claimed that staring too long into this central darkness could reveal glimpses of other realities—or drive the viewer permanently mad.

More advanced corporate shuttles prevented such episodes with sophisticated systems—lights that pulsed in gentle guidance, ceiling displays providing visual synchronization cues, hull stabilizers that dampened the worst of the transition shudders. This independent vessel offered none of these luxuries, just raw transition that separated the experienced from the novice.

"Thank you," Gress whispered, her voice unsteady, corporate composure temporarily abandoned. "I don't usually... that is..."

"No shame in it," Pro replied, his tone neither patronizing nor particularly sympathetic. "Corporate shuttles have stabilization systems. Indies run raw. First time's always rough."

He glanced around the cabin, noting that several of the Purist passengers had not weathered the transition well. Two were receiving emergency medical attention, their consciousnesses temporarily displaced by Dilation Hysteria. The religious man with the medallion sat with glazed eyes, technically conscious but experiencing the profound temporal dissociation that often triggered conversion to Purist ideology. Many of the Prophet's most fervent followers had first encountered his teachings while in this vulnerable state.

Relief washed over Gress's features, and within moments, she slipped into the coma-like state that most passengers entered during deep FTL travel—a protective mechanism of the mind against the fundamental wrongness of faster-than-light existence.

With the shuttle now fully transitioned, most passengers unconscious, Pro found himself oddly alert. Usually, he prioritized rest during these trips to compensate for his chronic sleep deficiency, but something felt off. The drive's hum carried an unfamiliar note, a barely perceptible dissonance that shouldn't exist in a properly aligned system.

He closed his eyes, focusing his specialized hearing. There it was—a sort of echo, resonating through his chest cavity with unsettling precision. Not a mechanical issue like a loose ferro-clasp vibrating against the hull or drive mountings, but something more fundamental. Something in the pulse itself.

FTL technology operated on a carefully calibrated principle: Cicillian's drive pulses were purposefully designed to create dissonance with the natural Vankh resonance. This precise misalignment propelled vessels across vast distances in fractions of the time required by conventional engines. But this shuttle's dissonance pattern contained an additional layer, an aberration he couldn't quite identify.

What would happen, Pro wondered, if a drive were attuned to sync perfectly with the Vankhs instead of opposing them? The thought had never occurred to him before, and he filed it away for later consideration. His expertise extended only to couplings, not the theoretical physics of drive design. This professional limitation had always frustrated him—once assigned to a specialization, a technician was biologically coded to that path, their standardized identifier determining everything from credit access to travel permissions.

During his third year at the Academy, Pro had attempted to cross-train in pulse harmonics—a discipline that would have complemented his coupling expertise and potentially opened a path to full drive engineering. The system had physically prevented him from accessing the training modules, his genetic coding triggering seizure-inducing feedback when he'd attempted to override the restrictions. Corporate specialization wasn't just professional pigeonholing; it was biological enforcement of designed limitations.

In just a few pulses, he'd reach Nexus Station and face the increasingly dangerous journey to his parents' planet. The Prophet's followers grew more zealous with each Vankh-Sett, their weapons becoming more sophisticated despite their supposed rejection of technology. The irony wasn't lost on Pro—how readily they embraced military hardware while denouncing medical enhancements as heresy.

The last communication from his father had been particularly disturbing. The Purists had begun implementing "purification centers" where those with enhancements were given a choice: voluntary removal or "spiritual rehabilitation"—a euphemism for procedures that often left the subject brain-damaged but technically alive. Those with simple augmentations might survive with only scars and decreased functionality, but integrated technologies like his mother's cortical implants—fused with her neural pathways over decades of use—were another matter entirely. Removal meant death or profound brain damage; non-removal meant persecution. It was a death sentence disguised as spiritual choice.

For now, though, Pro remained suspended between destinations, caught in transit like the FTL drive itself—neither fully here nor there, existing in the threshold space governed by the rhythm that ruled them all. He stared into the Vankh's Eye beyond the viewport, its perfect darkness somehow familiar, as though it recognized him just as surely as he recognized it.
# Chapter 2: Correspondence

The shuttle emerged from FTL with a violent shudder that sent Pro's diagnostic tools clattering against the deck plates. Metal scraped against reinforced polymer as the vessel's reality alignment fought to stabilize, each convulsion accompanied by the groaning protest of hull plates stressed beyond design parameters. The independent shuttle's lack of corporate-grade dampening systems made every transition a brutal reminder of the forces they routinely defied.

*Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

Pro reversed his breathing pattern with practiced precision, his neural interfaces flickering as they recalibrated from FTL synchronization back to standard time perception. Around him, passengers gasped and clutched their restraints, some still trapped in the disorienting aftermath of transit. The corporate woman across the aisle—Gress—remained pale as alabaster, her chrome implants flickering erratically as their quantum processors struggled to reconcile normal space-time with the temporal echoes still cascading through her enhanced visual cortex.

"Arrival at Nexus Station," announced the automated system, its voice carrying the slight distortion that marked independent vessels—corporate shuttles used AI personalities so sophisticated they were indistinguishable from human speech, while indies made do with synthesized approximations. "Local Vankh synchronization in progress. Please maintain respiratory discipline until docking is complete."

Pro shifted in his seat, feeling the familiar ache in his elongated spine that came from extended travel in ships designed for baseline human proportions. His evolved anatomy—generations of adaptation to low-gravity environments—had never been fully accommodated by independent vessel manufacturers. Corporate shuttles included ergonomic adjustments for the various evolutionary branches of humanity, but indies prioritized function over comfort.

Outside the viewport, Nexus Station materialized from the distortion like a fever dream made manifest. The massive ring structure dominated the barren moon's orbit, its outer hull a chaotic patchwork that told the story of humanity's fractured civilization. Corporate sections gleamed with polished efficiency—seamless curves of advanced polymers that seemed to flow rather than having been constructed. Independent additions jutted at harsh angles, utilitarian blocks welded to the ring's framework with purely functional aesthetics. Between them, Purist enclaves remained deliberately dim, their surfaces textured with the rough stone facades that rejected the smooth artificial materials favored by other factions.

*As above, so below,* Pro thought, observing how the station's physical architecture mirrored the philosophical divisions that structured their entire civilization. Each faction had literally built their worldview into the station's superstructure, creating a three-dimensional map of ideological conflict.

The docking procedure began with the low harmonic hum that preceded Vankh synchronization. Pro felt the vibration in his bones—a resonance that had become so fundamental to existence that most never questioned its origin. Through his enhanced hearing, he detected the subtle disharmony he'd noticed during transit, a secondary pattern that seemed to echo the primary pulse like a whispered conversation beneath a shout.

Three full Vankh cycles elapsed before the magnetic clamps engaged—significantly longer than standard procedure. Pro's professional assessment confirmed his earlier suspicions about the shuttle's condition. The captain had been pushing his equipment beyond safe parameters, trading mechanical integrity for schedule adherence. It was a gamble that worked until it didn't, and when it failed, passengers paid the price in temporal displacement or worse.

As the airlock sealed and passengers began their disembarkation, Pro shouldered his tool bag and approached Gress, who sat rigid in her seat, chrome implants still cycling through recalibration sequences.

"First time on an indie?" Pro asked, though her discomfort made the answer obvious.

"Corporate executive transport has certain... amenities," she replied, her voice carrying the careful modulation of someone fighting off residual Dilation Hysteria. "I wasn't prepared for the... authenticity of independent travel."

Pro nodded toward the exit where passengers were queuing for departure processing. "Station protocols are different here too. There are two main circulation pathways once you clear customs—green for corporate affiliates, orange for Purist-sympathizers. You'll want to stay green. Orange sections can be... unwelcoming to people with visible augmentations."

Gress touched her chrome implants instinctively, the metallic surfaces catching the station's ambient lighting. "I thought Nexus was neutral territory."

"Officially, yes. Reality tends to be more complicated." Pro adjusted his corporate badge, ensuring it remained visible but not prominent. "The station maintains neutrality by segregating rather than integrating. Keep your corporate credentials visible in green sections, and avoid orange zones entirely unless you have business there."

She gathered her belongings with the careful movements of someone whose inner ear was still adjusting to stable gravity. "What about you? Which path do you take?"

"Maintenance routes, mostly. We keep the infrastructure running that makes both sides possible." Pro gestured toward a discrete access panel near the passenger exit. "Different world down there."

As they joined the disembarkation queue, Pro studied his fellow passengers with professional interest. The Purist adherents moved in tight formations, their modified clothing designed to conceal the scars where technological enhancements had been removed. One elderly man's sleeve had shifted, revealing the distinctive purple-and-silver discoloration that marked where neural interfaces had been surgically severed—a procedure that often left lasting nerve damage but demonstrated ideological purity.

The corporate passengers maintained individual spacing, their enhanced sensory systems providing constant environmental updates that required minimal conscious attention. Pro watched data streams flow across their visual fields—information layers invisible to unaugmented eyes but clearly present to those with the appropriate hardware. Transit schedules, station maps, commercial notifications, security updates—a constant flow of contextual information that corporate citizens absorbed as naturally as breathing.

*The Purists fight against something they can't even see,* Pro realized, observing the stark difference in information access. The adherents navigated by physical signage and spoken announcements while corporate travelers moved with the confidence of those who possessed comprehensive environmental awareness. How could two groups find common ground when they literally inhabited different informational realities?

The airlock completed its cycle, and passengers moved into Nexus Station's arrival terminal. The space was designed to accommodate the various enhanced and natural variants of humanity—ceiling heights that worked for elongated frames, lighting spectrums that suited modified eyes, atmospheric mixtures that supported different respiratory enhancements. Yet despite these accommodations, tension filled the air like an electrical charge before a thunderstorm.

Pro noticed the security checkpoints immediately—corporate scanners that performed biometric analysis while appearing to be simple walkthrough arches. His neural interfaces provided real-time updates on the screening process: genetic markers confirmed, enhancement inventory catalogued, travel history cross-referenced, threat assessment calculated. The procedure was so seamless that most passengers remained unaware of its thoroughness.

Purist passengers underwent different screening protocols at separate stations—manual inspections by uniformed personnel who checked for concealed technology while maintaining respectful distance from religious sensitivities. The disparity was stark: corporate citizens experienced invisible, instantaneous processing while Purists endured visible, time-consuming searches that emphasized their status as potential security risks.

"Technician P-R-0-4-7-9," a voice called from a corporate processing station. Pro approached the scanner, allowing his enhanced identification systems to handshake with station security. His neural implants provided authentication tokens while biometric scanners confirmed his genetic markers and enhancement inventory.

"Three pending maintenance contracts registered," the security AI continued. "Corporate oversight authorization required for independent vessel service."

This was new. Pro's previous visits to Nexus had never required specific authorization for indie work. "What's the oversight procedure?"

"Report to Maintenance Supervisor L-I-N-3-8-2, Corporate Administrative Level 7, within one station cycle. Failure to comply will result in contract suspension and potential disciplinary review."

The name appeared in Pro's visual display along with a location marker and scheduling interface. Supervisor Lin—he'd never worked under her authority before, though her mathematical designation suggested expertise in precision systems. Corporate policy had apparently tightened regarding independent contractor oversight, another sign of the growing tensions between factions.

"Acknowledged," Pro replied, accepting the mandatory appointment. Whatever bureaucratic hoops required jumping, he needed those indie contracts to afford his parents' medications.

The arrival terminal's main concourse revealed Nexus Station's true character—a massive circular space where the different pathways diverged like arteries from a heart. The green corporate route curved upward toward the station's outer ring, its surfaces gleaming with embedded data displays and automated service interfaces. The orange Purist path descended toward the inner sections, marked by simple directional signage and natural lighting that avoided technological excess.

Pro bypassed both main routes, using his technical credentials to access a maintenance corridor that ran parallel to the primary circulation systems. Here, the station's engineered facade fell away, revealing the complex infrastructure that maintained the illusion of seamless operation. Conduits carried power, data, atmosphere, waste, and a dozen other essential services through passages designed for utility rather than aesthetics.

"Technician P-R-0-4-7-9," greeted the maintenance AI as he entered the service network. "Shuttle LX-291 priority inspection confirmed. Berth 47-C, Maintenance Level 3. Estimated completion time: four hours."

"Override to priority sequence," Pro transmitted through his neural interface. "Begin with critical harmonic analysis."

He made his way through the maintenance tunnels, passing other technical specialists who acknowledged him with the subtle nods of those who kept civilization functioning while remaining invisible to its beneficiaries. Their enhanced anatomies varied based on specialization—thermoreceptive modifications for heat management technicians, enhanced tactile sensitivity for precision assembly workers, electromagnetic perception for power systems specialists. Each had been shaped by their profession, their bodies optimized for functions that baseline humans couldn't perform efficiently.

The service passages offered glimpses into the station's true nature through maintenance viewports—corporate sections that operated with algorithmic precision, Purist areas where human labor replaced automated systems, and independent zones that combined technologies from both approaches with practical improvisation. The visible architecture represented mere surface manifestation of deeper philosophical divisions.

*Above and below,* Pro thought, observing how the station's public spaces concealed the essential infrastructure that made them possible. Corporate citizens saw seamless efficiency while remaining unaware of the maintenance workers who provided it. Purists experienced their simplified environments without recognizing the technological systems that enabled them. Only those who worked in the hidden spaces understood how the different factions depended on infrastructure they either ignored or actively rejected.

Berth 47-C housed the shuttle he'd recently traveled on, its hull bearing the accumulated damage of extended independent operation.
