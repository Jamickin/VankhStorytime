# Chapter 1

_Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

Pro synchronized his breathing pattern with the deep, resonant thunder that would soon crack through the shuttle's cabin. Around him, other passengers performed the same respiratory ritual, their enlarged, bi-pupiled eyes half-closed in concentration. The ambient lighting dimmed to a soft blue, designed to ease the transition into FTL travel.

Pro's elongated fingers drummed against his thigh, counting the seconds until departure. The acrid smell of recycled air, tinged with the metallic tang of drive lubricant, filled his nostrils. The passengers shifted nervously as the overhead lights continued to dim, halting at a barely visible hue.

"Commencing FTL initiation," announced the automated system, its synthetic voice oddly melodic against the background hum of the engines. "Pulse synchronization recommended."

_Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

He recited the technique in preparation for the Vankh Pulse, the familiar mantra settling into his muscles like an old friend. Around him, some whispered the pattern aloud, their voices creating a quiet chorus of breathwork.

Pro scanned the passenger cabin with practiced efficiency. Three rows ahead, a cluster of Purist adherents huddled together, their faces tense. Their appearance marked their philosophical stance—dextrous, slender digits and adapted eyes like everyone else, but deliberately stripped of all technological enhancements. Several bore the telltale scarring where implants had been surgically removed, leaving distinctive pockmarked patterns on their temples and wrists.

A Cicillian corporate policy officer sat nearby, his silver-threaded uniform marking him as regulatory compliance, cybernetic enhancements visibly integrated along his jawline. He watched the Purists with thinly veiled disdain, the kind reserved for those who chose philosophical objection over practical advancement.

The specialized tool bag beside him clinked softly as the shuttle vibrated, its coupling diagnostic equipment worth more than what most technicians earned in a full Vankh-Sett. Pro ran a protective hand over its worn surface, feeling each scratch and dent like a memory.

The corporate logo on his shoulder patch—a stylized "C" with three radiating lines representing Cicillian Industries—contrasted with the unmarked shuttle. These independent vessels had been multiplying throughout the sector recently, their hulls bearing the scars of hasty repairs and unofficial modifications as they operated beyond Cicillian's watchful gaze. His official assignment had ended two stops before, but instead of returning to the corporate hub on Nexus Station, he'd quietly accepted three more off-the-books coupling inspection jobs.

"Damn corporation," he muttered, exhaling slowly.

He needed those credits. His parents' last message still echoed in his mind—their voices tight with fear as they described how the Church of Purity had tightened its grip on medical supplies in the Helix Habitat. "Spiritual certification," they called it, a bureaucratic barrier between the sick and their treatment. The Church had banned everything from crude Motion Enhancers to complex Sensory Augmentations, declaring all technological assistance a "tainting of the flesh."

The data crysta she'd given him sat in his tool bag's secure compartment, unexamined. It's purple glow hidden. She'd pressed it into his hands with wild eyes, speaking of "suppressed histories" and "cosmic experiments," insisting he needed to understand what was really happening with the Vank network. Classic symptoms of neural desynchronizatio—the past and present blurring together, creating elaborate conspiracy theories. He'd taken it to humor her, but examining it would only feed her delusions. What she needed was proper medical care, not validation for paranoid fantasies.

His Pro's Mothe's condition was deteriorating, though she insisted it was just implant rejection—nothing temporal stabilizers couldn't handle. _Paranoid delusions,_ Pr reminded himself, pushing away the memory of her frantic warnings. _The implant rejection is affecting her cognitive functions._

_Right lung. Left lung. Right lung._

He felt the familiar tension in his strengthened tendons as his body prepared for acceleration. This would be his ninth transit this Vankh-Set—pushing the recommended safety limit. After years working with drive cores, Pr could feel the subtle dissonance from the FTL core in his chest cavity—a professional hazard that sometimes left him with temporal migraines.

"Those who Travel, Earn. Those who Earn, Survive," went the corporate creed, a hollow slogan repeated by managers who rarely left planetside comforts.

He remembered how proud he'd been when first accepted to the Cicillia Academy of Engineering. In those days—before the Church of Purit had gained political power—an appointment to the Academy had been the pinnacle of achievement for a habitat-born child. His parents had celebrated for days, his Pro's Fathe especially beaming with pride. After all, it had been his Pro's Fathe who had inspired Pr's fascination with drive technology, bringing home technical manuals from his shifts at the Cicillia maintenance facility.

"You'll have more opportunities than I did," his Pro's Fathe had said during Pr's departure celebration. "Full engineering clearance, not just maintenance routines." He had tapped his own damaged leg, the faulty Motion Enhancer visible beneath his uniform trousers. It had been that defective enhancement—an early model prone to neural rejection—that had ended his Pro's Fathe's career. Cicillia had "retired" him five Vankh-Sets before standard age, deeming him "efficiency-compromised."

The academy years had been filled with rigorous indoctrination. Corporate philosophy was embedded into everything from meal supplements to sleep-learning modules, which bypassed conscious thought to implant "historical context" directly into students' memory centers. "The Cicillia Corporation's founding mandate of 278AVestablished humanity's covenant with the Vankh network," the modules had repeated night after night. "Through dedicated sacrifice and innovation, our forebears harnessed the pulse that now allows civilization to span the stars."

_What a joke,_ Pr thought, as a subtle vibration resonated through the deck plates. He felt the warning signs of a migraine already building behind his specialized eyes, a pressure like distant thunder.

The specialization ceremony remained vivid in his memory. Final-year Academy students gathered in the grand auditorium as assessors announced assignments that would define their careers. Pr had known his aptitude scores placed him at the top percentile for full drive engineering—the elite track that granted access to the most closely guarded secrets of Vankh technology.

"Candidate P-R-0-4-7-9," the assessor had called, using his full designation. "Drive Coupling Specialist."

The words had fallen like blows. Not full engineering. Not theoretical design. Coupling specialist—a crucial role, but limited to a single component system. His protests were met with platitudes about "optimal resource allocation." Later, an instructor took pity on him and revealed the truth: his Pro's Fathe's "retirement" had flagged Pr's genetic profile. Corporate risk assessment algorithms had deemed him potentially "unstable for sensitive information access" based on nothing more than hereditary association.

_...Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

"First time on an indie transport?"

Pr looked across the narrow aisle to see a woman with standard neural enhancement ports gleaming at her temples. "This is my first time traveling independent. Is that... normal?" She gestured toward the rattling seats, wincing as a strong vibration shuddered through the cabin.

He glanced at her corporate attire—too pristine, too new. "Ship's running hot," he answered, keeping his voice low. "Coupling needs adjustment. Nothing dangerous, but they're pushing for speed." He tapped his corporate badge. "Don't mention I said anything."

The woman's chromed eyes widened, her iris-enhancements contracting nervously. She nodded, producing a small lanyard badge. "Stationary Specialist Gres," she introduced herself, her diction precise.

Upper management slumming it on an independent vessel? Unusual. Pr studied the standard Cicillia Industries tag.

"Drive Coupling Specialist P-R-0-4-7-9," he replied, his own voice carrying the slight roughness of a technician. "May the Pulse guide you."

"May the Pulse guide you," she echoed, settling back with visible relief, as though his certification alone might protect her.

An older man in the row opposite—clearly a Puris adherent by his simple clothing—scoffed audibly. "Corporate blessings," he muttered, "as if the Pulse belongs to Cicillia."

Pr recognized a sympathizer, though not a full convert—those wouldn't dare travel on FTL vessels at all. "The Pulse guides all who respect its rhythm," Pr replied neutrally, the corporate-approved response. The man turned away with a dismissive grunt.

Gress leaned closer, her voice barely audible. "Are there many... adherents traveling independent these days?"

Pr nodded slightly. "Corporate rates have tripled since the Helix situation. Many can't afford standard transport." He didn't add that these sympathizers often served as couriers, their anti-scanning garments making them immune to standard security checkpoints.

Pr's eyes drifted to his own hands, to the barely perceptible dermal reinforcement that protected against drive radiation and the neural interfaces at his wrist joints. Not choices, but prerequisites for his specialty, mandated and implemented during his first year at the Academy.

The shuttle's vibrations intensified. The coupling would show accelerated corrosion when he inspected it later; these independent operators always pushed their equipment too hard, not understanding how subtle misalignments shortened a component's lifespan exponentially.

Beyond the viewport, space began to distort. Stars stretched into lines of light, their colors shifting. Only Pr's specialized training and enhanced eyes allowed him to track these changes without the protective filters of corporate vessels. This independent shuttle offered no such luxuries.

When not on official duty, Pr had built a quiet reputation among independent pilots who couldn't afford a corporate specialist. This particular shuttle was a fascinating hybrid—a Cicillia hull married to a third-party drive system, a technical heresy that corporate inspectors would condemn immediately. His last illicit job had nearly ended in disaster during a surprise dock check by corporate security. Pr had hidden in a maintenance shaft for six hours while they cataloged every component. The captain later revealed that corporate sabotage of independent vessels had become increasingly common—a pattern that coincided with the Church of Purit's growing influence.

What PRO didn't mention was how his diagnostic readings had been showing increasingly bizarre patterns lately. Mathematical relationships so precise they seemed designed. Harmonic frequencies that matched no known physical constants yet maintained perfect stability. Readings that suggested conscious control rather than chaotic physics.

_Prfessional paranoia,_ he told himself. _Too much time listening to my Pro's Mothe's theories._

**VVVVVANKH!**

The Vankh Puls thundered through the cabin, a vibration that seemed to resonate in the marrow of his bones. The pulse triggered the final breathing sequence, and Pr's thoughts turned involuntarily to his parents.

His Pro's Mothe had once overseen the habitat's historical archives, her cortical enhancements allowing her to process thousands of documents daily. She had been renowned for her knowledge of pre-Vankh exploration records—a subject mysteriously restricted shortly before the Church gained power. Now, she was forced to hide her enhancements, the implants slowly failing without proper maintenance. They weren't simple augmentations but complex neural interfaces integrated with her brain over decades.

They were still living in the prestigious Helix Habita, now proclaimed the divine birthplace of the Prphet. The Church had transformed his childhood home into a fortress against "technological corruption." Zealots patrolled the streets, and priests conducted daily "purification" ceremonies with incense in the Lux-Zones where he'd once played with his Pro's Mothe.

During Pr's last visit, the anti-aircraft batteries had nearly shot his shuttle down. His Pro's Mothe had shown signs of neural degradation—moments of confusion, her aging enhancements conflicting with her natural cognitive processes. The medications she needed were now classified as "spiritually corrupting."

His Pro's Fathe's face seemed to age Vankh-Sets every time they met, worn down by the daily rituals and the stress of managing an underground rebellion. The corporation had abandoned him long ago, cutting off his enhancement maintenance. Now his movement enhancer, fused to his bone, caused constant pain as it degraded. The jagged line where metal met bone was visibly inflamed.

"We're managing," his Pro's Fathe had whispered during their last meeting. "The Prphet's Voice announced another cleansing ceremony next pulse. They're searching for hidden tech."

Outside the viewport, reality fragmented. The black void between stars took on texture—rippling patterns that most were trained to ignore. These distortion fields resembled the Vankh spheres themselves—perfect black orbs that dotted settled space, emitting the pulses that made FTL travel possible. No one understood what they were, only that civilization had learned to harness their rhythm.

**VVVVVANKH!**

The second pulse hit harder. The familiar sensation of reality stretching washed over him, held at bay by his disciplined breathing. His neural calibrators filtered the visual anomalies—streaking stars and fluctuating color spectrums that could drive the unprepared mad. Beyond the standard distortions, Pr noticed something unusual in the void—a secondary ripple pattern that seemed to echo the primary pulse. In his eight Vankh-Sets of transit work, he'd never observed this phenomenon.

A strangled gasp from across the aisle pulled his attention to the present. The corporate woman—Gress—was struggling, her breathing faltering as panic overtook her. A sheen of sweat glistened on her forehead, her chromed eyes dilated fully, reflecting the chaotic light. The early symptoms of Dilation Hysteria flickered across her features. The Puris with the medallion had gone rigid, his eyes rolled back to whites, the crude scar tissue where he'd removed his neural stabilizers leaving him vulnerable.

Her vision must be swimming, Pr realized. Cascades of shapes, temporal confusion, vertigo, and then the violent Snap-Back that could leave permanent neural damage.

"Amateur," Pr whispered. He began tapping his foot against the deck plates, the metallic _click-click-click_ cutting through the hum of the drives, an anchor of pattern.

Just before the Snap-Back could take hold, her breathing steadied, following his lead. The panic receded. After a tense moment, the shuttle shuddered one final time as reality fully transitioned. The view outside became a swirling tunnel of blue-shifted light surrounding an absolute void at the center. Technicians like Pr knew it by a different name: the Vankh's Eye. Legend claimed that staring too long into this darkness could reveal glimpses of other realities—or drive a viewer permanently mad.

"Thank you," Gress whispered, her voice unsteady.

"No shame in it," Pr replied. "Corporate shuttles have stabilization systems. Indies run raw. First time's always rough." Within moments, she slipped into the coma-like state most passengers entered during deep FTL travel.

With the shuttle transitioned, Pr found himself oddly alert. The drive's hum carried an unfamiliar note, a dissonance that shouldn't exist. He activated his portable diagnostic unit, running passive scans on the coupling system. The readings populated his display—temperature, harmonics, resonance. He closed his eyes, focusing. There it was—an echo, resonating with unsettling precision. Not a mechanical issue, but something in the pulse itself.

The patterns were… wrong.

Not dangerous-wrong, but mathematically-impossible-wrong. The harmonic resonance showed perfect golden ratio relationships across seventeen different frequency bands. The thermal distribution followed Fibonacci sequences that should never occur in chaotic mechanical systems. The temporal displacement readings created geometric progressions that demanded conscious design.

Pr stared at the data. This wasn't random physics. This was engineered precision disguised as a natural phenomenon.

_Pro's Mothe's paranoia is contagious,_ he told himself, but the readings didn't lie.

He closed the diagnostic display. Some questions were better left unasked, especially when the answers might prove his supposedly delusional Pro's Mothe had been right all along. The shuttle continued its journey, its course plotted by navigation systems that corrected for stellar drift patterns that—according to his readings—didn't actually exist.

In just a few pulses, he'd reach Nexus Statio and face the dangerous journey to his parents' planet. The last communication from his Pro's Fathe had been disturbing. The Puriss had begun implementing "purification centers." Those with enhancements had a choice: voluntary removal or "spiritual rehabilitation"—a euphemism for procedures that often left the subject brain-damaged. For integrated technologies like his Pro's Mothe's cortical implants, removal meant death. Non-removal meant persecution. A death sentence disguised as spiritual choice.

For now, Pr remained suspended between destinations, caught in the threshold space governed by the rhythm that ruled them all. He stared into the Vankh's Eye beyond the viewport, its perfect darkness somehow familiar, as if it recognized him.

He maintained his breathing pattern, trying not to think about data crystas, impossible mathematics, or the growing certainty that his Pro's Mothe's "delusions" might be the most accurate perception of reality anyone had achieved in generations. The universe hummed around him with perfect, terrible precision.

**VVVVVANKH!**

The pulse resonated through his consciousness, no longer just a navigation beacon but a question he wasn't ready to answer: _In a universe where mind created matter, whose mind was doing the creating?_

---

The shuttle flew on through the infinite dark, carrying its passengers toward destinations they thought they had chosen, following routes that had been planned before they were born, synchronized to a rhythm that counted down to something none of them understood.
