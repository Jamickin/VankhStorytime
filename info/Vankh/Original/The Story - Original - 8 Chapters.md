

# VANKH: Rhythms of Reality
## Chapter 1: The Rhythm

*Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

Pro synchronized his breathing pattern with the deep resonant VVVVVANKH that pulsed through the shuttle's cabin. Around him, other passengers performed the same respiratory ritual, their enlarged bi-pupiled eyes half-closed in concentration. The ambient lighting dimmed to a soft blue, designed to ease the transition into FTL travel.

"Approaching FTL initiation," announced the automated system. "Pulse synchronization recommended. Initiation commences on the third Vankh."

Pro's specialized tool bag rested between his feet, the coupling diagnostic equipment inside worth more than what most technicians earned in a full Vankh-Sett. The corporate logo on his shoulder patch—a stylized "C" with three radiating lines representing Cicillian Industries—contrasted with the unmarked shuttle he'd boarded. These independent vessels had been multiplying throughout the sector, operating in the margins of corporate oversight. His official assignment had ended two pulses ago, but instead of returning to corporate headquarters, he'd accepted three more coupling inspection jobs.

The credits would buy medicine for his parents. Their messages had grown increasingly desperate as the Church of Purity tightened its grip on medical supplies, requiring "spiritual certification" before dispensing treatment.

*Right lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

He felt the familiar tension in his strengthened tendons as his body prepared for acceleration. This would be his fourth transit this Vankh-Sett—pushing the recommended safety limit. The subtle vibration from the FTL core beneath the passenger cabin resonated at a frequency slightly out of sync with the true Vankhs. After years working with drive cores, Pro could feel the dissonance in his chest cavity—a professional hazard that sometimes left him with temporal migraines after long shifts.

"Pardon, technician." A slender woman with chromed eye implants leaned across the aisle. "First time traveling independent. Is this... normal?" She gestured toward the ceiling where the lighting flickered slightly out of sync with the Vankh pulse.

"Ship's running hot," Pro answered, keeping his voice low. "Coupling needs adjustment. Nothing dangerous, but they're pushing for speed." He tapped his corporate badge. "Don't mention I said anything."

The woman nodded, her implants catching the blue light. Clearly upper management from some corporate enclave, slumming it on an independent vessel. Probably thought it was adventurous.

The pilot was pushing this shuttle too hard. Pro could tell from the harmonic undertones in the drive's pulse—the coupling would show accelerated corrosion when he inspected it. He made a mental note to recommend a fifteen percent reduction in acceleration parameters. These independent operators always tried to squeeze extra speed from outdated equipment, never understanding how the subtle misalignment affected the drive's integrity.

What they couldn't know—what nobody knew—was how their attempts to break speed records were automatically reported through the very pulses they generated. Pro's diagnostic readings, uploaded to the corporate database, unwittingly mapped every vessel trying to push beyond established parameters. He simply thought he was monitoring equipment safety; he couldn't know his work helped maintain the elaborate boundaries of their cosmic prison.

VVVVVANKH!

The second pulse triggered the final breathing sequence. Pro thought of his parents, living in what was once the prestigious Helix Habita, now proclaimed the divine birthplace of The Prophe. The Church of Purity had transformed his childhood home into a fortress against "technological corruption," their zealots patrolling the curved streets where he once played. The last time he'd smuggled himself planetside, the anti-aircraft batteries had nearly detected his specialized stealth pod.

His father's face had aged decades in just a few Vankh-Setts, worn down by the daily "purification rituals" now required of all habitat residents. His mother's eyes had lost their spark, the cortical enhancement scars on her temples now disguised under the mandatory head coverings.

"We're managing," his father had whispered, glancing nervously at the door. "The Prophet's Voice announced another cleansing ceremony next pulse. They're searching for hidden tech."

VVVVVANKH!

The third pulse arrived. Pro completed his breathing cycle as the FTL drive fully engaged. The familiar sensation of reality stretching washed over him—the beginnings of Dilation Hysteri held carefully at bay by his disciplined respiratory pattern. His enhanced eyes adjusted to the temporal distortion, the specialized pupil structure filtering the visual anomalies that accompanied FTL travel.

Across the aisle, the corporate woman faltered in her breathing sequence, her eyes widening in momentary panic. Pro reached over instinctively, tapping her wrist in the rhythm she needed to follow. A sheen of sweat broke across her forehead as she struggled to synchronize, the early symptoms of Dilation Hysteria flickering across her features. After a tense moment, she found the pattern, her breathing stabilizing as the shuttle's reality fully transitioned to FTL.

"Thank you," she whispered, visibly shaken. "I don't usually... that is..."

"No shame in it," Pro replied. "Corporate shuttles have stabilization fields. Indies run raw."

As the shuttle slipped into the deeper phase of faster-than-light travel, Pro reviewed his plans for the coming pulses. Three more coupling inspections, then the dangerous journey to his parents' planet. The Prophet's followers grew more zealous with each Vankh-Sett, their weapons more sophisticated despite their supposed rejection of technology. The irony wasn't lost on Pro—how readily they embraced weapons while denouncing medical enhancements as heresy.

The woman beside him had fallen into the light trance most passengers adopted during FTL travel, but Pro remained alert, attuned to the subtle harmonics of the drive beneath them. Something about the oscillation pattern bothered him—a barely perceptible dissonance that shouldn't be present in a properly aligned system.

In two pulses, he'd be back home, navigating the treacherous boundary between corporate space and Purist territory. For now, though, he was still in transit, caught between worlds like the FTL drive itself—neither fully here nor there, suspended in the rhythm that governed them all.

## Chapter 2: The Illusion

The shuttle emerged from FTL with a violent shudder that sent Pro's diagnostic tools clattering against the deck. Around him, passengers gasped and clutched their restraints as the vessel's reality alignment stabilized with a series of smaller convulsions.

*Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung.*

Pro reversed his breathing pattern, easing his body back into standard time. The corporate woman across the aisle was pale, her chrome implants flickering as they recalibrated to normal space. First-timers always struggled with the transition.

"Arrival at Nexus Statio," announced the automated system. "Local Vankh synchronization in progress. Please maintain respiratory discipline until docking is complete."

Outside the viewport, Nexus Station materialized from the distortion—a massive ring structure orbiting a barren moon. Its outer hull was a patchwork of corporate logos and independent additions, reflecting the station's status as a neutral trading hub where Corporate and Purist interests reluctantly coexisted. 

The docking procedure took three Vankh pulses—longer than standard, which confirmed Pro's suspicions about the drive's condition. As passengers disembarked, he shouldered his tool bag and nodded to the corporate woman.

"Follow the green pathway for corporate transit," he advised. "The blue sections aren't safe for someone with visible augmentations."

She touched her chromed implants self-consciously. "I thought Nexus was neutral territory."

"The maps say so." Pro adjusted his corporate badge to be less prominent. "Reality disagrees."

The arrivals corridor was a cacophony of competing advertisement projections, holographic vendors, and the constant undercurrent of the Vankh pulse that synchronized all station activities. Corporate citizens with their sleek enhancements moved in coordinated patterns, while those from Purist-influenced territories clustered in tight groups, their unaugmented eyes darting suspiciously at the technological displays.

Pro bypassed the main concourse, swiping his tech certification at a maintenance access point. The door hissed open, revealing a sparse corridor where the station's pretenses fell away—exposed conduits, repair drones, and the raw infrastructure that kept the illusion of prosperity functioning.

"Technician P-R-0-4-7-9," greeted the station's maintenance AI. "Three pending coupling inspections registered. Priority sequence loaded to your corporate interface."

"Override sequence," Pro replied. "Begin with shuttle LX-291. Arrived seventeen minutes ago with critical harmonic dissonance."

The AI paused, its programming weighing corporate protocols against potential drive failure. "Override accepted. Shuttle LX-291 moved to priority position. Berth 47-C."

Pro made his way through the maintenance tunnels, passing other technicians who acknowledged him with the subtle nod of those who kept the machinery of civilization running while remaining invisible to its beneficiaries. The station's true pulse was here—not in the commercial hubs or diplomatic sectors, but in these narrow corridors where reality was maintained one repair at a time.

Berth 47-C housed the shuttle he'd just traveled on. Its captain, a grizzled man with faint Maldar heritage evident in the crystalline patterns beneath his skin, waited impatiently beside the exposed drive coupling.

"Corporate sent someone fast," he remarked, surprise evident in his tone. "Didn't expect service for at least a full pulse."

"Not here officially," Pro replied, kneeling to examine the coupling. The corrosion patterns confirmed his suspicions—micro-fractures in the harmonic oscillator, temporal displacement in the pulse synchronization. "You've been pushing beyond safety parameters."

The captain's expression hardened. "We've got schedules to keep. Not all of us travel with corporate protection."

Pro ran his diagnostic scanner over the coupling, watching as the readings confirmed what his senses had already detected. "Pushing it won't matter if your drive fails mid-transit. You'd trap your passengers in perpetual dilation."

The horror of such a fate hung between them—bodies frozen between moments, experiencing the eternal stretching of time without release. The Vankh pulse that provided their measure of reality would become an eternal, unending torture.

"Can you fix it?" The captain's voice had lost its edge.

Pro nodded, already unpacking his specialized tools. "I can realign the harmonic resonance, replace the corroded segments. But you need to reduce acceleration by seventeen percent. Non-negotiable."

As he worked, Pro's mind drifted to his parents, picturing the medicine they desperately needed. Each credit from these unofficial jobs brought him closer to affording the specialized anti-rejection compounds his mother required for her now-illegal cortical implants. The Purists would rather see her brain degrade than permit the "technological corruption" that had once been standard medical care.

Three hours later, the coupling gleamed with new components, its pulse now perfectly aligned with the station's Vankh rhythm. Pro encoded the maintenance record with his personal signature rather than his corporate identifier—a small deception that kept these side jobs invisible to Cicillian Industries.

"She'll hold now," Pro told the captain, accepting the credit transfer with a discreet scan of his subdermal chip. "Keep to the seventeen percent reduction."

The captain nodded, though both men knew he'd likely push the limits again once beyond corporate space. "Heading anywhere after your work here?"

Pro hesitated. Discussing travel to Purist territories invited questions. "Just standard inspection rounds."

"If you need transport that doesn't ask questions," the captain offered, "we depart in two pulses. Our flight path skirts the edge of the Helix Syste."

The mention of his home system sent a jolt through Pro's carefully maintained composure. "I might take you up on that."

"Berth 47-C. Two pulses from now." The captain tapped his own temple in the subtle gesture of those offering services outside corporate oversight. "We understand the need for discretion."

Pro completed his remaining inspections with mechanical efficiency, his mind already plotting the risky journey home. The transport paths to Helix were increasingly monitored as the Prophet's influence spread. Direct corporate flights were denied entry, while independent vessels faced intense scrutiny and often "purification" of any crew or passengers with visible augmentations.

His final inspection complete, Pro made his way to Nexus Station's obscure lower level where an unlicensed augmentation specialist operated behind the facade of a nutrition dispensary. The bell chimed as he entered, triggering a scan that identified him as neither corporate enforcement nor Purist zealot.

The back room revealed the specialist's true operation—a clinical space with advanced medical equipment that would be the envy of many corporate facilities. The specialist herself, a woman with such extensive modifications that her original features were unrecognizable, greeted Pro with a knowing smile.

"The coupling technician returns," she observed. "Another delivery for the family?"

Pro placed his corporate badge on the scanner, allowing the specialist access to his accumulated credit balance. "The usual package, plus something for temporal degradation if you have it."

She frowned. "Your mother's condition is worsening?"

"The last message mentioned lost time. Days disappearing." Pro kept his voice steady despite the fear twisting inside him. "The Purists call it divine punishment for her past 'corruption.'"

The specialist turned to her supply cabinets, fingers dancing across locked panels that responded to her unique biometric signature. "I have something experimental. Not corporate approved, but effective for cases like hers."

She produced a small container with iridescent capsules that seemed to shift phases as Pro watched. "Temporal stabilizers. Developed for deep space miners experiencing chronic dilation exposure. Four thousand credits."

More than all his unofficial work this cycle would earn. Pro hesitated, then nodded. "Add it to the standard package."

As the specialist prepared the smuggling container—designed to appear as maintenance supplies to casual scans—Pro studied the shadow of his reflection in the polished surface of a medical cabinet. His enhanced eyes, the strengthened tendons visible beneath his skin, the subtle neural port at his temple—all markers of corporate modification that would need to be concealed before he entered Purist territory.

"You should consider not going back," the specialist said quietly as she worked. "Each trip increases your risk profile. The Prophet's followers are becoming more sophisticated in their detection methods."

"They're my parents," Pro replied simply.

She sealed the container and handed it to him. "The universe runs on many rhythms, technician. The Vankh is just the one we've chosen to follow."

The heretical implication hung in the air—that the pulse that governed their lives, synchronized their technology, and measured their existence was somehow... optional. Such thoughts were dangerous in corporate space and blasphemous in Purist territories.

Pro secured the container in a hidden compartment of his tool bag. "I leave in two pulses. Independent transport."

"May your rhythms align," the specialist offered the traditional traveler's blessing.

As Pro made his way back toward the docking berths, he passed through the station's central atrium where a massive holographic display showed the positions of the nearest Vankh sphere. Travelers of all factions gathered to watch the synchronized pulses that governed faster-than-light travel throughout known space.

VVVVVANKH!

The deep resonant pulse washed through the station, and for that brief moment, all activity synchronized—Corporate and Purist, enhanced and natural, wealthy and desperate—all bound by the same fundamental rhythm that made their civilization possible.

None of them questioning why the black spheres existed. None of them wondering what purpose the pulse truly served. All of them embracing the illusion that had been crafted for them since before recorded history.

Pro watched the display, feeling the familiar resonance in his chest. Two more pulses, and he would risk everything to help his family. Beyond that... the patterns of his life continued as they always had, measured by the eternal rhythm that none of them truly understood.

## Chapter 3: The Causality

"Identification," demanded the Helix System border patrol, their unmarked vessel displaying none of the Purist symbols that would have revealed their true allegiance. The captain of Pro's transport—the independent shuttle LX-291—transmitted the falsified manifest that identified them as suppliers of agricultural equipment.

Pro sat in the cargo hold, sealed inside a maintenance pod ostensibly containing atmospheric regulators. His corporate identifiers and augmentations were masked by a thin layer of temporal-reflective coating that disrupted scanning technologies. The coating itched against his skin, a constant reminder of the danger he faced.

Through the pod's audio feed, he heard the patrol officer's skeptical tone. "Agricultural equipment? The Prophet has declared self-sufficiency throughout Helix."

"Different kind of cultivation," the captain replied smoothly. "The lower habitats still grow the medicinal herbs for the Prophet's purification ceremonies. We bring specialized soil composition analyzers."

A tense silence followed, broken by the border patrol's grudging clearance. "Proceed to Habitat Ring designation four-seven-nine. Deviation from approved flight path will result in immediate neutralization."

Pro felt the shuttle's slight course adjustment, the familiar vibration of the newly-repaired coupling maintaining perfect alignment with the Vankh pulse. The mention of Habitat 479 sent a wave of relief through him—it was the ring adjacent to his parents' home. The captain had managed to secure approach clearance remarkably close to his destination.

An hour later, sealed within a cargo container labeled "Soil Composition Analysis Unit," Pro was transported into the habitat's receiving bay. The Purist enforcers performed only a cursory scan before waving the shipment through—their technological capabilities deliberately limited by their own doctrine yet still dangerous to anyone carrying contraband.

Once inside the storage facility, Pro waited for the three quick taps that signaled the departure of the inspection team. The container's hidden release activated, and he emerged into the dim lighting of the supply room. The captain's contact—a middle-aged woman with the distinctive facial scarring of those who had removed their augmentations—gestured for him to follow.

"Move quickly," she whispered. "Curfew begins at the next pulse."

Pro followed her through service corridors once used for automated maintenance, now repurposed as smuggling routes. The habitat's transformation was even more pronounced than during his last visit. Where vibrant holographic displays had once illuminated the curved walkways, now only dim natural lighting remained. Residents walked with downcast eyes, their clothing uniformly plain, any previous enhancement scars carefully hidden beneath high collars and long sleeves.

"The Prophet addressed the central congregation yesterday," his guide informed him as they navigated the back passages. "Declared another wave of purification coming. Those still harboring technology will face public cleansing."

"Cleansing?" Pro asked, though he feared he already knew.

"They remove augmentations without anesthesia," she replied flatly. "As a lesson."

They reached a junction where the service corridor met a residential sector. His guide pointed down a narrower passage. "Third dwelling on the right. They're expecting you. I'll return before first pulse to guide you out."

The residential corridor was unnervingly silent compared to Pro's memories of music, conversation, and the background hum of technology that had once filled these spaces. No children played in the common areas, no neighbors gathered for communal meals. Every door was shut, the habitat's former community fractured into isolated units of fear.

At the third dwelling, Pro entered his family's identification code—his mother's birth date combined with his father's original corporate designation. The door slid open just enough for him to slip inside before closing immediately behind him.

"Pro." His mother's voice broke as she embraced him, her frame noticeably thinner than during his last visit. The required head covering couldn't completely hide the inflammation around her temple implants—the rejection his medicine would hopefully alleviate.

His father emerged from the bedroom, moving with the careful deliberation of someone managing chronic pain. "You shouldn't have come. The risks increase with each visit."

"I brought what you need." Pro removed the hidden compartment from his tool bag, revealing the medicine package. "Including something new for the temporal symptoms."

His mother's eyes widened. "How did you afford temporal stabilizers? They're worth more than—"

"It doesn't matter," Pro interrupted, not wanting them to worry about his financial situation. "The specialist said four doses over the next two Vankh-Setts should reverse the degradation."

As his father examined the medicine, Pro took in the changes to their dwelling. The comfortable home of his childhood was now austere to the point of barrenness. All evidence of their former corporate status had been removed or hidden. Where his father's prized holographic art collection had once rotated on the main wall, now hung only the mandatory symbol of the Prophet—an open eye with a pupil shaped like the Vankh sphere.

"They've increased the inspections," his father said, carefully hiding the medicine inside a false panel they had constructed beneath the food preparation unit. "Three this past Vankh-Sett alone. The dwelling across the corridor was emptied last pulse. The family had hidden a corporate education module for their child."

"What happened to them?" Pro asked.

His parents exchanged a glance loaded with unspoken horror. "The parents were sent for cleansing," his mother finally answered. "The child was taken to the Prophet's educational enclave."

The implications chilled Pro. Children separated from "corrupted" parents were rarely returned, instead raised in the Prophet's doctrine to become the most zealous enforcers of Purist ideology.

"Tell me about the Prophet," Pro said, keeping his voice low despite the sound dampeners his father had illegally maintained in the dwelling's walls. "The news doesn't reach corporate territories clearly."

His father's expression darkened. "He was nobody until five Vankh-Setts ago. A maintenance worker in the upper habitats. Claims he experienced a 'revelation' during an FTL transit when his breathing regulator malfunctioned."

"Dilation Hysteria," Pro suggested. The temporal distortion experienced without proper breathing protocols often produced hallucinations and religious epiphanies.

"Perhaps," his father conceded. "But he emerged with a compelling narrative—that the Vankh pulse isn't a natural phenomenon that enables our civilization, but a divine rhythm we've corrupted with technology."

His mother continued the explanation. "He preaches that augmentations and FTL travel distort our perception of the 'true pulse.' That humanity's modifications are preventing us from hearing some greater message in the Vankh rhythm."

Pro frowned. "That's not entirely inconsistent with Cicillian Churc doctrine. They also teach that the Vankh network carries deeper significance."

"The difference," his father said bitterly, "is that corporations want to enhance our connection to the network, while the Prophet demands we sever it. Both claim to know the truth, both demand absolute loyalty."

A subtle vibration ran through the habitat—the precursor to the coming Vankh pulse. Pro's parents instinctively aligned their breathing, a lifetime of conditioning impossible to break even under Purist rule.

VVVVVANKH!

As the pulse resonated through the habitat, Pro noticed something unusual—a secondary harmonic, almost imperceptible, that he had never detected before. His specialized training made him uniquely attuned to variations in the Vankh rhythm, and this subtle dissonance triggered his professional concern.

"Did you feel that?" he asked when the pulse faded.

His parents looked confused. "Feel what?" his father asked.

"A harmonic distortion in the pulse. Like an echo, or..." Pro struggled to describe the sensation. "Like something responding to the Vankh."

His mother's expression grew worried. "You should rest. The border crossing and the temporal coating can cause sensory anomalies."

Pro didn't argue, but made a mental note of the phenomenon. In his years working with drive couplings, he had developed an intuitive understanding of the Vankh pulse's properties. This variation seemed significant, though he couldn't yet understand why.

While his mother prepared a meal from their limited rations, Pro helped his father with the dwelling's maintenance—fixing the aging atmospheric regulator, reinforcing the hidden compartments that concealed their remaining technology, and checking for surveillance devices that might have been planted during recent inspections.

"How long can you stay?" his father asked as they worked.

"Until next pulse," Pro replied. "The transport leaves shortly after."

His father nodded, resignation evident in his features. "We've been discussing leaving the habitat. Finding a way off-planet."

Pro paused in his work, surprised. His parents had always refused evacuation, claiming their age and his mother's condition made relocation too dangerous. "What changed?"

"The Prophet is planning something," his father whispered, glancing toward the dwelling's entrance. "There are rumors of a 'Final Purificatio.' All remaining technology to be removed or destroyed. All those with irreversible modifications to be... eliminated."

The clinical language couldn't disguise the horror of what was being proposed—mass execution of those whose augmentations could not be removed without causing death. Including his mother, whose neural implants had become too integrated with her brain tissue for safe extraction.

"I can arrange transport," Pro said immediately. "Corporate territories would grant you asylum based on my employment status."

His father shook his head. "The border controls tighten every pulse. Your mother couldn't survive the necessary concealment procedures, and I..." he gestured to his leg, where Pro knew an early-generation mobility enhancement was permanently fused to his bone structure. "I wouldn't pass even the most basic scan."

"Then I'll find another way," Pro insisted. "There are independent operators who specialize in extracting high-risk individuals from Purist territories."

The conversation paused as his mother called them for the meal—a simple protein base with the few vegetables still permitted under Purist restrictions on "artificially enhanced" agriculture. They ate in the careful silence of those accustomed to surveillance, speaking only of innocuous topics that would raise no alarms if overheard.

After the meal, Pro administered the first dose of temporal stabilizer to his mother. The iridescent medication seemed to shimmer as it dissolved against her tongue, its advanced components already beginning to counteract the degradation around her implants.

"It tastes like sunlight," she murmured, surprise evident in her voice. "I can feel the missing days returning."

Pro monitored her vital signs using a disguised diagnostic tool from his kit, relieved to see the inflammation markers decreasing. "The specialist said you might experience temporal echo—memories becoming more vivid. It's a side effect, but not harmful."

His mother's eyes, clearer than they had been in several Vankh-Setts, fixed on him with sudden intensity. "I remember something the Prophet said before the restrictions began. About the Vankh spheres."

Pro leaned closer, intrigued. "What about them?"

"He claimed they aren't communication relays as the corporations teach. He called them 'the seals upon our prison.'" She shook her head. "At the time, I dismissed it as the ravings of a maintenance worker suffering from Dilation Hysteria. But now I wonder..."

The familiar vibration signaled another approaching pulse. Pro automatically synchronized his breathing, noticing how his parents now suppressed their respiratory patterns—the Purist doctrine requiring them to "resist the false rhythm."

VVVVVANKH!

Again, Pro detected the subtle harmonic distortion—a secondary wave pattern underlying the primary pulse. This time, he was certain it wasn't his imagination or a side effect of the temporal coating. Something was responding to the Vankh network, a phenomenon he had never observed in corporate territories.

As the evening progressed, Pro shared carefully edited stories of his work, omitting details that might endanger his parents if forced to report their conversations. They, in turn, updated him on the habitat's transformation—former neighbors who had embraced Purist ideology, friends who had disappeared during "cleansings," the dismantling of educational and medical facilities deemed "excessively technological."

When his father finally retired to rest, exhaustion evident in his careful movements, Pro's mother lingered in the common area, her eyes more focused than he had seen in several visits.

"There's something I need to show you," she whispered, glancing toward the bedroom to ensure his father was not listening. "Something I've kept hidden even from him."

She led Pro to the dwelling's small hygiene station, activating the water recycler to mask their conversation from potential surveillance. From behind a wall panel, she extracted a small data crystal—technology so forbidden under Purist rule that mere possession carried a death sentence.

"Before the Prophet's rise," she explained, "I worked in the habitat's historical archives. During the first wave of purges, when they began destroying 'corrupting information,' I saved what I could."

Pro stared at the crystal. "What's on it?"

"Historical anomalies," she replied. "Records of the Vankh network that contradict both corporate and Purist narratives. Observations from early space exploration, before the pulse became the foundation of our civilization."

She pressed the crystal into his hand. "I can't access this anymore—my remaining tech is dedicated to keeping my implants from killing me. But you, in corporate territory, might find answers." Her eyes met his, suddenly intense. "The Prophet is insane and dangerous, but his core claim—that the Vankh network isn't what we've been told—might hold some truth."

Pro carefully secured the crystal in a hidden compartment of his diagnostic tool. "I'll find a secure terminal and examine it."

A soft alert chimed from the dwelling's entrance—a warning that curfew would begin with the next pulse. Pro embraced his mother, feeling her fragility and strength simultaneously.

"I'll find a way to get you both out," he promised. "Before this 'Final Purification' begins."

She cupped his face in her hands, studying him with the piercing gaze that had intimidated him throughout childhood. "Be careful, Pro. Some questions aren't meant to be answered. Some rhythms aren't meant to be broken."

The guide returned as promised, leading Pro back through the service corridors as the habitat's lighting dimmed for the enforced curfew period. They moved silently, avoiding the Purist patrols that swept through the residential sectors searching for technology violations.

As they approached the cargo area where his transport waited, Pro noticed increased activity—additional security, cargo being rerouted, inspection protocols more rigorous than during his arrival.

"What's happening?" he whispered to his guide.

"The Prophet made an announcement during final prayer," she replied tersely. "All off-world transports will cease after three more pulses. The habitat is sealing itself from 'external corruption.'"

Pro felt a chill of urgency. If the habitat sealed itself completely, extracting his parents would become nearly impossible. The timeline for their escape had just compressed from vague future planning to immediate necessity.

The shuttle captain looked relieved when Pro finally boarded, sealed once again in the maintenance pod. "Cutting it close," he muttered. "Two more minutes and we'd have lost our departure window."

As the shuttle lifted from the habitat's docking port, Pro felt the data crystal pressing against his side, its secrets waiting to be revealed. His mother's words echoed in his mind: *Some rhythms aren't meant to be broken.*

Yet even as the thought formed, the subtle harmonic distortion he had detected during the Vankh pulses seemed to respond—a discordant note in the perfect rhythm that governed their existence, a crack in the foundation of their reality.

The shuttle accelerated away from Helix, carrying Pro back toward corporate space and the next phase of his increasingly complicated life. Behind him, the habitat containing his parents grew smaller, its enforced isolation a preview of the coming severance from the wider system.

What he couldn't know—what none of them could know—was how the causality of their actions rippled through layers of reality they couldn't perceive. How his possession of the data crystal, his detection of the harmonic distortion, and his determination to rescue his parents would converge to create a fracture in the carefully maintained prison of their existence.

Three pulses from now, everything would change. The path to that change had just been set in motion, inevitable as the next VVVVVANKH.

## Chapter 4: The Perception

The corporate checkpoint scanned Pro thoroughly upon his return, the automated systems searching for contraband, pathogens, or unauthorized technology that frequently accompanied travelers from Purist territories. Pro stood in the decontamination chamber, arms extended, as the blue light washed over him in rhythmic pulses synchronized to the Vankh network.

"Anomalous substance detected," announced the system in its neutral tone. "Dermal layer contains temporal-reflective particles."

Pro had anticipated this. "Standard protection for coupling technicians entering radiation zones," he explained, presenting his corporate credentials. "Form T-79 authorization for specialized protective coatings."

A moment of processing silence followed. "Authorization verified. Temporal-reflective coating logged for scheduled removal within two pulses. Proceed to secondary screening."

The coating that had hidden him from Purist scanners would now become a liability in corporate space if not removed on schedule—the substance itself wasn't illegal, but its continued presence would trigger increasingly invasive inquiries. Pro made a mental note to visit the corporate medical center before his next shift.

Secondary screening involved a living inspector—a heavily augmented woman whose enhancements were clearly designed for security functions. Multiple scanning apparatus extended from her modified arms, and her eyes shifted through visual spectrums as she assessed Pro.

"Purpose of visit to Helix System?" she asked, though her scanners had likely already determined the answer.

"Unofficial," Pro replied carefully. "Family residence."

She nodded, unsurprised. "Increasing numbers of corporate citizens attempting family extraction from Purist territories. Cicillian Industries offers relocation services for immediate family members of valued employees." Her tone shifted to the practiced cadence of a corporate advertisement. "Would you like to schedule a consultation with our Extraction Specialists?"

Pro hesitated. Official extraction would mean documentation, surveillance, and corporate oversight—dangerous for his parents given their hidden technology and his mother's unstable condition. "I'll consider the option," he answered noncommittally.

The inspector's enhanced eyes analyzed his reaction, clearly noting his reluctance but choosing not to flag it. "Proceed to decontamination completion. Your scheduled shift begins in seven pulses."

Pro collected his belongings—thoroughly scanned but returned intact, the data crystal safely concealed within his diagnostic tool's redundant power supply compartment. The corporate transportation system whisked him from the checkpoint to the residential sector allocated for his employment tier, the automated pod traveling along routes that pulsed with the same rhythm as everything else in their civilization.

His dwelling in Corporate Habitat C-12 was a stark contrast to his parents' home—sleek, technology-integrated, and constantly connected to the Cicillian network. As he entered, the environmental systems activated, adjusting to his preferred settings.

"Welcome back, Technician P-R-0-4-7-9," greeted the dwelling's AI. "You have seventeen messages pending. Priority notification from Maintenance Supervisor L-I-N-3-8- regarding upcoming assignment."

"Display priority message," Pro instructed, setting down his tool bag and beginning the process of unpacking.

The supervisor's image appeared on the main display wall. "Pro, upon your return, report for special assignment briefing. Cicillian's received a high-priority contract servicing the pulse relay stations near the Vankh sphere in Sector . Your coupling expertise is specifically requested. Message ends."

Pro froze momentarily. Service assignments near actual Vankh spheres were rare and typically reserved for the most specialized technicians. The massive black orbs that emitted the civilization-sustaining pulse were usually given wide berth, their immediate vicinity restricted to only essential maintenance.

More significantly, Sector 7 bordered the Helix System. This assignment could provide legitimate corporate access to the region where his parents were trapped.

But first, he needed to examine the data crystal. Pro prepared carefully, activating his dwelling's privacy protocols—officially for rest purposes, unofficially creating a brief window where his activities wouldn't be automatically logged to the corporate monitoring system.
