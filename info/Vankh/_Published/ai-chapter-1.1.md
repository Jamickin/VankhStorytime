# Chapter 1.1: The Departure

"Everything flows, out and in; everything has its tides; all things rise and fall."

---

_Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

Pro synchronized his breathing pattern with the deep, resonant thunder that would soon crack through the shuttle's cabin. Around him, other passengers performed the same respiratory ritual, their enlarged, bi-pupiled eyes half-closed in concentration. The ambient lighting dimmed to a soft blue, designed to ease the transition into FTL travel.

His elongated fingers drummed against his thigh, counting the seconds until departure. The acrid smell of recycled air, tinged with the metallic tang of drive lubricant, filled his nostrils. The passengers shifted nervously as the overhead lights continued to dim, halting at a barely visible hue.

"Commencing FTL initiation," announced the automated system, its synthetic voice oddly melodic against the background hum of the engines. "Pulse synchronization recommended."

_Left lung. Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

He recited the technique in preparation for the Vankh Pulse, the familiar mantra settling into his muscles like an old friend. Around him, some whispered the pattern aloud, their voices creating a quiet chorus of breathwork—a collective ritual that had become as fundamental to human existence as speech itself.

Pro scanned the passenger cabin with practiced efficiency, his enhanced eyes cataloguing the social dynamics that played out in miniature aboard every independent vessel. Three rows ahead, a cluster of Purist adherents huddled together, their faces tense with the effort of following the breathing pattern. Their appearance marked their philosophical stance—dextrous, slender digits and adapted eyes like everyone else, but deliberately stripped of all technological enhancements. Several bore the telltale scarring where implants had been surgically removed, leaving distinctive pockmarked patterns on their temples and wrists like badges of ideological purity.

A Cicillian corporate policy officer sat nearby, his silver-threaded uniform marking him as regulatory compliance, cybernetic enhancements visibly integrated along his jawline. The modifications pulsed with soft bioluminescence as they processed environmental data streams invisible to unenhanced eyes. He watched the Purists with thinly veiled disdain, the kind reserved for those who chose philosophical objection over practical advancement.

The specialized tool bag beside him clinked softly as the shuttle vibrated, its coupling diagnostic equipment worth more than what most technicians earned in a full Vankh-Sett. Pro ran a protective hand over its worn surface, feeling each scratch and dent like a memory. The corporate logo on his shoulder patch—a stylized "C" with three radiating lines representing Cicillian Industries—seemed to mock him with its pristine stitching against the unmarked shuttle.

"Damn corporation," he muttered, exhaling slowly through the prescribed pattern.

These independent vessels had been multiplying throughout the sector recently, their hulls bearing the scars of hasty repairs and unofficial modifications as they operated beyond Cicillian's watchful gaze. His official assignment had ended two stops before, but instead of returning to the corporate hub on Nexus Station, he'd quietly accepted three more off-the-books coupling inspection jobs.

The credits were essential. His parents' last message still echoed in his mind—their voices tight with fear as they described how the Church of Purity had tightened its grip on medical supplies in the Helix Habitat. "Spiritual certification," they called it, a bureaucratic barrier between the sick and their treatment. The Church had banned everything from crude Motion Enhancers to complex Sensory Augmentations, declaring all technological assistance a "tainting of the flesh." The same zealots who had once praised such advancements now labeled them heretical.

The data crystal his Pro's Mother had given him sat in his tool bag's secure compartment, unexamined. Its purple glow remained hidden behind reinforced shielding. She'd pressed it into his hands with wild eyes, speaking of "suppressed histories" and "cosmic experiments," insisting he needed to understand what was really happening with the Vankh network.

Classic symptoms of neural desynchronization—the past and present blurring together, creating elaborate conspiracy theories to explain her deteriorating condition. He'd taken it to humor her, but examining it would only feed her delusions. What she needed was proper medical care, not validation for paranoid fantasies born from failing neural implants.

_Right lung. Left lung. Right lung._

He felt the familiar tension in his strengthened tendons as his body prepared for acceleration. This would be his ninth transit this Vankh-Sett—pushing well beyond the recommended safety limit. The subtle vibration from the FTL core beneath the passenger cabin resonated at a frequency slightly out of sync with true Vankhs, and after years working with drive cores, Pro could feel the dissonance in his chest cavity—a professional hazard that sometimes left him with temporal migraines lasting for days.

"Those who Travel, Earn. Those who Earn, Survive," went the corporate creed, a hollow slogan repeated by managers who rarely left planetside comforts.

The academy years felt like a lifetime ago now. He remembered the pride when first accepted to the Cicillian Academy of Engineering—in those days, before the Church of Purity had gained political power, an Academy appointment had been the pinnacle of achievement for a habitat-born child. His parents had celebrated for days, his Pro's Father especially beaming with pride.

"You'll have more opportunities than I did," his Pro's Father had said during Pro's departure celebration. "Full engineering clearance, not just maintenance routines." He had tapped his own damaged leg, where the faulty Motion Enhancer was visible beneath his uniform trousers—an early model prone to neural rejection that had ended his career when Cicillian deemed him "efficiency-compromised."

The specialization ceremony remained vivid in his memory. Final-year Academy students gathered in the grand auditorium, the air thick with anticipation as corporation assessors announced assignments that would define their entire careers. Pro had known his aptitude scores placed him at the top percentile for full drive engineering—the elite track that would grant access to the most closely guarded secrets of Vankh technology.

"Candidate P-R-0-4-7-9," the assessor had called, using his full designation rather than the abbreviated name he preferred. "Drive Coupling Specialist."

The words had fallen like blows. Not full engineering access. Not theoretical design work. Coupling specialist—a crucial role, certainly, but limited to a single component system. His protests had been met with corporate platitudes about "optimal resource allocation" and "specialized excellence."

Later, he'd discovered the truth from an instructor who'd taken pity on him: his Pro's Father's "retirement" had flagged Pro's genetic profile. Corporate risk assessment algorithms had deemed him potentially "unstable for sensitive information access" based on nothing more than hereditary association.

_...Left lung. Pause. Right lung. Exhale. Reverse._

"First time on an indie transport?"

Pro looked across the narrow aisle to see a woman with standard neural enhancement ports gleaming at her temples. The chrome implants caught the blue light, reflecting it in prismatic patterns across her face. "This is my first time traveling independent. Is that... normal?" She gestured toward the rattling seats and buckles, wincing as a particularly strong vibration shuddered through the cabin.

He glanced at her corporate attire—too pristine, too new. The faint scent of expensive synthetic fabric confirmed his suspicion: executive class, unused to anything less than luxury transport.

"Ship's running hot," he answered, keeping his voice low enough that it wouldn't carry beyond their conversation. "Coupling needs adjustment. Nothing dangerous, but they're pushing for speed." He tapped his corporate badge with a conspiratorial gesture. "Don't mention I said anything."

The woman's chromed eyes widened, iris-enhancements contracting nervously. She nodded, reaching into her coat pocket to produce a small lanyard badge. "Stationary Specialist Gress," she introduced herself, her diction precise and clipped in the way of those who rarely ventured beyond corporate enclaves.

Upper management slumming it on an independent vessel? Unusual. Pro studied the standard Cicillian Industries tag as she held it out. Perhaps she thought it adventurous, or more likely, she'd discovered the significant price difference between corporate and independent transport.

"Drive Coupling Specialist P-R-0-4-7-9," he replied, his own voice carrying the slight roughness common among technicians who spent too much time inhaling drive coolant. "May the Pulse guide you."

"May the Pulse guide you," she echoed, settling back into her seat with visible relief, as though his technical certification alone might protect her from the ship's questionable maintenance.

An older man in the row opposite—clearly a Purist adherent by his simple clothing and the religious medallion visible at his collar—scoffed audibly at their exchange. "Corporate blessings," he muttered just loudly enough to be heard, "as if the Pulse belongs to Cicillian."

Pro had encountered this attitude often enough to recognize a sympathizer, though not a full convert—those wouldn't dare travel on FTL vessels at all, considering it blasphemous interference with the divine rhythm. This man represented the dangerous middle ground—resentful of corporate control but not yet radicalized to violent rejection of technology.

"The Pulse guides all who respect its rhythm," Pro replied neutrally, the corporate-approved response to religious provocations. The man turned away with a dismissive grunt.

Gress leaned closer, her voice barely audible above the increasing engine vibrations. "Are there many... adherents traveling independent these days?"

Pro nodded slightly. "Corporate rates have tripled since the Helix situation. Many can't afford the prerequisites for standard transport." He didn't add that these sympathizers often served as information couriers between growing cells throughout corporate space, their anti-scanning garments making them immune to standard security checkpoints.

The shuttle's vibrations intensified, and Pro's trained ear detected the harmonic undertones in the drive's pulse. The coupling would show accelerated corrosion when he inspected it later—these independent operators always pushed their equipment too hard, never understanding how subtle misalignments affected drive integrity. They squeezed extra speed from outdated technology without comprehending the exponential cost to component lifespan.

Around them, the diverse passengers represented the fractured state of their civilization in microcosm. Corporate citizens with their seamless technological integration, Purist adherents bearing the scars of ideological purity, independents pragmatically accepting whatever worked regardless of philosophical consistency. Each group navigating the same physical space while inhabiting fundamentally different conceptual realities.

The lighting dimmed further as the shuttle's systems prepared for final departure protocols. Pro closed his eyes, feeling the rhythm of his own heartbeat synchronize with the building engine resonance. His enhanced respiratory system—modified during Academy training for optimal FTL performance—automatically adjusted to the changing atmospheric conditions.

Beyond the viewport, space itself began to show the preliminary distortions that preceded FTL transition. Stars took on subtle halos, their light bending through gravitational fields that the drive systems were beginning to manipulate. Only Pro's specialized training and enhanced eyes allowed him to perceive these changes without the protective filters that corporate vessels automatically deployed.

This independent shuttle offered no such luxuries—passengers either possessed appropriate training or suffered the psychedelic effects of unfiltered transition preparation. He watched several Purist passengers already showing signs of discomfort, their unenhanced nervous systems struggling with the subtle reality distortions that accompanied the approach to faster-than-light travel.

"Pre-transition phase initiated," the automated system announced. "Final pulse synchronization commencing in sixty seconds."

The moment had arrived. Pro settled deeper into his seat, his breathing pattern now perfectly synchronized with the building resonance. Around him, other passengers completed their own preparations—some confident in their training, others fighting rising panic as their bodies responded to forces beyond normal human experience.

His thoughts turned briefly to his parents, still trapped in Helix Habitat under the Church of Purity's increasingly oppressive control. His Pro's Mother with her failing neural implants, speaking of cosmic conspiracies while her enhanced mind slowly desynchronized from reality. His Pro's Father with his degrading motion enhancer, managing an underground resistance while corporate medicine remained beyond their reach.

The credits from these independent jobs would buy them another month of black market medications. Another month of hope that somehow the situation would improve, that the zealots would lose power, that medical care would become available again for those with enhancement rejection syndrome.

 The engine resonance reached critical threshold. Pro felt it in his bones, in his modified nervous system, in the technological components that had become inseparable from his biological identity. The familiar mixture of anticipation and unease that preceded every FTL jump, multiplied by the uncertainty of traveling on an independent vessel with questionable maintenance standards.

"Pulse synchronization achieved," the system announced. "FTL transition commencing."

Pro completed his breathing pattern one final time, his enhanced physiology preparing for the fundamental violation of causality that was faster-than-light travel. Around him, the other passengers braced themselves in their own ways—corporate citizens trusting in their technological modifications, Purist adherents embracing the raw experience of cosmic forces, independents simply hoping their pragmatic acceptance would prove sufficient.

The moment stretched, reality holding its breath.

Then—

**VVVVVANKH!**
