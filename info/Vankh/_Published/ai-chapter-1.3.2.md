# Chapter 1.3.2: The Orange Corridors

He told himself it was intelligence-gathering. That a technician with new eyes should observe everything, including what he'd been trained to avoid. That there was nothing irrational about turning right instead of left when the disembarking queue split at the customs threshold and the [[Cicillian]] concourses opened in blue ahead of him and the [[Purist]] enclaves waited in amber and dim behind him.

He turned right.

The orange corridors had their own smell — stone dust and something medicinal and the faint copper undertone of old wound-dressings. The people who moved through them wore their choices plainly: the puckered tracks of [[Church of Purity]] removals along jawlines and forearms and temples, the stiffness of those who had returned flesh to its baseline and were still relearning what baseline meant. No chrome implants here. No calibration LEDs pulsing beneath skin. Nothing augmented, nothing optimised, nothing that would make you easier to measure.

They looked at his eyes first — his enhanced eyes, which he could not hide, which had just shown him something the designers never intended him to see. He understood, in a new and colder way, why they looked there first.

No one asked him to leave. He was simply watched.

What he found was not what he had expected to find. He had come looking for the fanaticism that the corporate briefings described and found instead something more uncomfortable: a kind of exhaustion, the specific exhaustion of people who had made a desperate choice in a moment of clarity and were now living inside it permanently, holding it steady against doubt by the pressure of being together. They prayed in small clusters near a sealed service corridor. They shared food from unlabelled containers. They did not discuss doctrine because doctrine was for the comfortable — here, the fact of being here was the argument.

An older woman, her hands shaking with the tremor of a recent removal, caught his gaze and held it. No hostility. Only attention.

"You've seen something," she said.

"Yes."

"So had he." She did not say [[The Prophet]]'s name. She didn't need to. "That's what they don't put in the testimonies. Revelation doesn't resolve anything. It doesn't grant peace or certainty or a simpler life. It only makes the thing you were looking at visible. Everything difficult that follows — that's yours."

[[Pro]] returned to the blue concourses before the dwell-time on his transit log could read as deliberate. He did not know, precisely, what he had found in the orange section. Only that the woman had described something he recognised. And that it fit — the way an instrument reading fits when all the other readings have suddenly snapped into alignment — into the shape of the larger thing he was beginning to understand.
