# Chapter 2.1: The Illusion

"As above, so below; as below, so above."

---

The shuttle emerged from [[FTL]] with a violent shudder that sent [[Pro]]'s diagnostic tools clattering against the deck plates. Metal scraped against reinforced polymer as the vessel's reality alignment fought to stabilize, each convulsion accompanied by the groaning protest of hull plates stressed beyond their design parameters. The independent shuttle's lack of corporate-grade dampening turned every transition into a brutal reminder of the forces they routinely defied.

_Right lung. Left lung. Left lung. Right lung._

[[Pro]] reversed his breathing pattern with practiced precision, the neural calibrators at his temples flickering as they dragged his perception back out of transit-time and into the slow, heavy now of normal space. Around him the cabin came apart into noise—gasps, the rattle of restraints, the wet sound of someone being sick three rows down. Across the aisle, [[Gress]] sat rigid and alabaster-pale, her chromed implants stuttering through recalibration sequences, their light fracturing across her face in colors that didn't belong to any spectrum the cabin's lamps could produce.

"Arrival at [[Nexus Station]]," announced the automated system, its voice carrying the slight synthetic burr that marked an independent vessel. Corporate shuttles spoke with AI personalities indistinguishable from a living host; indies made do with approximations. "Local Vankh synchronization in progress. Maintain respiratory discipline until docking is complete."

[[Pro]] shifted, feeling the familiar ache settle into his elongated spine—the price of long hours folded into seats designed for baseline proportions. His evolved anatomy, the inheritance of generations adapted to low-gravity habitats, had never been a thing independent manufacturers bothered to accommodate. Comfort was a corporate luxury. Function was what the rest of them could afford.

Beyond the viewport, [[Nexus Station]] resolved out of the distortion like a fever made architecture. The vast ring dominated the orbit of its barren moon, and its outer hull told, in welded scars and seams, the whole fractured story of their civilization. [[Cicillian]] sections gleamed—seamless curves of advanced polymer that seemed to flow rather than to have been built. Independent additions jutted from the ring at hard, utilitarian angles, function bolted onto function with no thought of grace. And between them the [[Purist]] enclaves sat deliberately dim, their surfaces textured with rough stone facades that refused the smooth artificial materials of the other factions.

_As above, so below,_ [[Pro]] thought, watching the station's body announce the divisions that structured every life inside it. Each faction had built its worldview into the superstructure—a three-dimensional argument, rendered in metal and stone, that no one had ever won.

The docking procedure opened with the low harmonic hum that always preceded synchronization. [[Pro]] felt it climb through his bones, that resonance so fundamental to existence that most never thought to ask where it came from. And beneath it, threaded through his enhanced hearing, the thing he had carried out of transit and could not put down: a secondary pattern, faint as a whisper under a shout, echoing the primary pulse a half-beat behind—as though something out in the dark were answering it.

Three full [[Vankh-Sett]] cycles elapsed before the magnetic clamps engaged. Far longer than standard. His professional read of it was grim and certain: the captain had been pushing his equipment past safe parameters, trading the integrity of his [[drive coupling]] for the schedule. It was a gamble that paid until the moment it didn't, and when it failed it failed all at once, and the passengers paid the difference in temporal displacement—or worse.

As the airlock cycled and the queue began to shuffle forward, [[Pro]] shouldered his tool bag and crossed to where [[Gress]] still sat, her implants only now settling.

"Station's the same as the ship," he told her, low. "Two main circulation routes once you clear customs. Blue for [[Cicillian]] affiliates, orange for [[Purist]] sympathizers. Stay blue. The orange sections can be unwelcoming to anyone wearing visible augmentations."

[[Gress]] touched her chrome temples on reflex, the metal catching the ambient light. "I thought [[Nexus Station]] was neutral territory."

"Officially." [[Pro]] adjusted his corporate badge until it sat visible but unassertive. "Reality tends to be more complicated. The station keeps the peace by segregating rather than integrating. Keep your credentials showing in the blue concourses and stay out of the orange unless you've got business there."

She gathered her belongings with the careful, deliberate movements of someone whose inner ear had not yet conceded that the floor was holding still. "And you? Which route do you take?"

"The maintenance routes, mostly. We keep the machinery running that makes both sides possible." He nodded toward a discreet access panel near the passenger exit. "Different world, down there."

The arrivals corridor was a cacophony of competing advertisement projections, holographic vendors, and the constant undertow of the [[Vankh pulse]] synchronizing everything that moved. [[Cicillian]] citizens slid through the crowd in coordinated patterns, their enhanced senses feeding them streams of context invisible to anyone without the hardware to receive it—transit schedules, station maps, commercial overlays, security advisories, an entire informational reality layered over the physical one. The [[Purist]] travelers moved differently: in tight clusters, navigating by physical signage and spoken announcement, their unaugmented eyes flicking with suspicion at the technological displays.

_They fight against something they cannot even see,_ [[Pro]] realized, watching the gulf between the two. How could either group ever find common ground with the other when they did not, in any literal sense, inhabit the same world?

The security checkpoints announced themselves to his enhanced perception the moment he stepped into the terminal—corporate scanners disguised as simple walkthrough arches, performing biometric analysis so seamless that most passengers never knew they had been read. His own neural interfaces returned the procedure to him in real time: genetic markers confirmed, enhancement inventory catalogued, travel history cross-referenced, threat assessment calculated and filed. The [[Purist]] passengers, meanwhile, were funneled to separate stations for manual inspection—uniformed personnel checking for concealed technology, the searches visible and slow and humiliating in a way the corporate scans never were. One group processed in silence; the other made to stand and be seen as a risk.

"Technician P-R-0-4-7-9." The voice came from a corporate processing station as he passed. [[Pro]] approached, allowing his identification systems to handshake with station security. Authentication tokens, biometric confirmation, enhancement inventory—the whole transaction completed in the space of a breath.

"Three pending maintenance contracts registered," the security system continued. "Corporate oversight authorization now required for independent-vessel service."

That was new. In all his previous crossings, indie work had never needed specific authorization. "What's the oversight procedure?"

"Report to Maintenance Supervisor L-I-N-3-8-2, Corporate Administrative Level Seven, within one station cycle. Failure to comply will result in contract suspension and disciplinary review."

The name surfaced in his visual field alongside a location marker. Supervisor [[Lin]]—he had never worked under her authority, though the mathematical cast of her designation suggested a specialist in precision systems. Corporate policy had tightened around independent contractors while his back was turned. One more pressure, in a [[Vankh-Sett]] that was running short of room for them. "Acknowledged," he said, and accepted the appointment he could not refuse. He needed those contracts. His parents needed the medicine the contracts bought.

He bypassed both the blue and the orange and used his technical credentials to slip into a maintenance corridor running parallel to the public arteries. Here the engineered facade fell away and the station showed its true face: exposed conduits carrying power, data, atmosphere, and waste through passages built for utility and nothing else. Other specialists passed him with the subtle nod of those who keep a civilization alive while remaining invisible to the people it carries. Their bodies varied with their trades—thermoreceptive grafts for the heat-management crews, augmented touch for the precision assemblers, electromagnetic perception for the power technicians. Each had been shaped by the work, optimized for functions baseline flesh could not perform. The hidden labor that held up the seamless world above.

Berth 47-C held the shuttle he had ridden in on, its hull wearing the accumulated damage of long independent service. The captain—a grizzled man, the crystalline patterns of distant Maldari ancestry traced faintly beneath his skin—waited beside the exposed [[drive coupling]], his modifications flaring brighter with circulation as he caught sight of [[Pro]].

"Corporate's getting stricter," the captain said, gesturing at official monitoring equipment that had not been there on [[Pro]]'s last visit. "They're documenting everything now."

[[Pro]] knelt beside the coupling and began the initial diagnostics—and his eye snagged on the meteorite scarring along the hull beside it. He went still.

The damage was wrong. Completely, fundamentally wrong.

Everything he had ever been taught held that their vessels crossed dozens of light-years between systems, folding space-time through the [[Vankh spheres]]. Damage from that kind of travel should record the journey: debris fields from a dozen stellar regions, each with its own mineral signature, its own particle density, the whole varied history of deep space written into the pitting. Instead the scarring told a single, monotonous story. The mineral signatures were uniform—numbingly uniform—lacking any of the diversity that true interstellar passage would leave behind. Worse, the impact angles and frequencies spoke of near-light velocities, of ordinary matter striking ordinary matter. Ships moving faster than light should accumulate no such wounds at all; they existed, in transit, in a state that bypassed the universe entirely.

"How long has she been in service?" [[Pro]] asked, keeping his voice level.

"Twelve [[Vankh-Sett]]s of regular running," the captain said. "Corporate space to indie settlements, the odd haul out to [[Purist]] territory."

Twelve [[Vankh-Sett]]s. The scarring should have been a chronicle of a dozen regions, and instead it described a single small neighborhood of space—a few light-months, perhaps, dressed up in the flight logs as a galaxy. [[Pro]] filed the observation away where the monitoring equipment could not see his face, and felt the floor of the assumed world tilt quietly beneath him.

He thought of his mother's data crystal, its purple glow shut behind shielding in his tool bag. _The archives don't match,_ she had told him, eyes too bright. _The records, the timelines—none of it adds up to natural progression. Too perfect. Too controlled. Too authored._ He had taken the crystal to humor a failing mind. He was no longer certain whose mind had been failing.

The same logic that ran beneath the [[Vankh pulse]], beneath the impossible precision of the couplings he had scanned in transit, ran here too, in the dumb mute language of damaged metal. The scale of their civilization was a fiction. The distances were a fiction. Something had drawn a small box around them and painted the inside of it to look like the sky.

[[VVVVVANKH!]]

The pulse rolled through the berth, and every instrument in reach snapped briefly into alignment with it—the universal rhythm asserting itself through steel and bone alike. And under it, unmistakable now, that secondary harmonic: a half-beat behind, patient, answering.

It was then that he became aware of the presence at the mouth of the maintenance tunnel. A figure in corporate administrative attire, watching him work—watching, [[Pro]] understood with a cold certainty, the precise moment his face had changed. Mathematical enhancement patterns flickered beneath her skin like equations rearranging themselves, and her attention rested on him with the weight of something that had already begun to calculate.
